# §6 Parametric-Source Transformations (P6 + P7)

This chapter discusses two transformation pathways that take Parametric carriers as their source: P6 (Evaluator → Policy, transferring already-internalized assessment experience into Policy parameters) and P7 (Parametric → Tokenized, externalizing parametric experience into Token-level artifacts consumable by other systems).

## §6.1 Parametric Evaluator-to-Policy Transformation (P6)

P6 describes the following transformation: assessment experience solidified in parametric Evaluators is migrated into Policy parameters. The source carrier is Evaluator parameters—the weights of a trained reward model, process reward model, parametric verifier, critic, judge, or value model; the target carrier is Policy parameters. The distinction from P5 (Tokenized → Policy) lies in the intermediary through which supervision passes: P5 directly writes raw trajectories, human demonstrations, or environmental success signals into the Policy; P6's supervision must be refracted through the learned assessment capability of a parametric Evaluator before entering the Policy.

Strictly speaking, the evaluated $(c, a, o)$ is the original source of the experience; the Evaluator has only compressed one facet of that experience ("what kind of output is better") into its own weights. The P5/P6 boundary falls on the intermediary through which supervision passes, not on "where the experience came from." This Survey takes Evaluator parameters as the source carrier in P6—they are simultaneously the product of P4 (Tokenized → Evaluator) and the starting point of P6.

P6's defining characteristic is that the Evaluator is not merely invoked by the agent at inference time but persistently alters Policy parameters through training signals. If an Evaluator only serves as an inference-time controller during decoding, reranking, best-of-N selection, tree search, or MCTS, and Policy weights are not updated by that signal, it constitutes evaluator utilization rather than P6. As soon as the Evaluator's output is converted into reward, preference pair, advantage, penalty, filtered trajectory, or refinement target and used to update Policy parameters, it constitutes P6.

**Boundary 1: Source limited to parametric Evaluators.** Rule-based verifiers (unit tests, exact math answer matching, compiler return codes) are zero-parameter functions whose signals are environment-grounded outcomes and belong to P5, not P6; only parametric verifiers (trained correctness-discrimination models) are legitimate P6 source carriers. When a work mixes parametric RMs with rule-based constraints—such as the verifiable-reward sub-setting in [Ack26] or the rule-based constraint judges in [Xu24d]—only the portions relying on parametric Evaluators constitute clean P6.

**Boundary 2: Off-the-shelf LLM-as-judge as boundary case.** A class of work directly uses general-purpose LLMs as judges ([Lee23b], [Guo24]). These judges' assessment capability primarily comes from pretraining, and is difficult—sometimes impossible—to trace back to a specific segment of agent experience, posing a challenge to the Experience Grounding condition (§2.3, Condition 1). This Survey treats them as P6 boundary cases rather than excluding them. First, a prompted judge and a trained RM are structurally isomorphic in that "assessment capability is solidified in parameters and persistently influences Policy through training signals." Second, when a judge is constrained under a specific constitution, rubric, or meta-question—[Bai22b]'s constitution-guided feedback, [Ren26b]'s meta-evaluation—the evaluated $(c, a)$ together with the constraint provides experience grounding. Works involving off-the-shelf judges are explicitly marked with their boundary status in the text.

**Boundary 3: P5/P6 criterion when Evaluators filter or correct data.** A class of work uses Evaluators to filter or correct data and then performs SFT ([Don23], [Yua25c], [Zha26am]); the carrier that actually enters the Policy is the tokenized trajectory, superficially close to P5. Our attribution criterion is: if the Evaluator's judgment is the load-bearing factor that enables the data to be improved (without the Evaluator, these trajectories would neither be constructed nor selected), and the paper's core contribution lies in "how to use the Evaluator to filter or correct experience," it belongs to P6; if the Evaluator is only a peripheral component and the core lies in the source or form of the trajectories themselves, it belongs to P5.

**Classification axes.** This section organizes P6 methods along two orthogonal axes. The first is **feedback granularity**: Outcome (assessing complete outputs, full trajectories, or entire episodes) vs. Process (assessing intermediate steps, local actions, reasoning prefixes, tool-use steps, or state-action pairs). The second is **feedback form**: Discriminative (the Evaluator produces comparable numerical or ordinal signals—scalar reward, score, pairwise preference, chosen/rejected label) vs. Generative (the Evaluator produces explanatory or corrective natural-language content—critique, failure explanation, refined step, or revised trajectory). The two axes cross to yield four categories—discriminative outcome, generative outcome, discriminative process, generative process—corresponding to §6.1.1–§6.1.4. Attribution of a given work follows the primary feedback artifact that genuinely drives the policy update, not the Evaluator's richest internal capability, nor the training algorithm's name.

### §6.1.1 Discriminative Outcome Evaluator-to-Policy Transfer

Discriminative outcome evaluators produce scalar rewards, trajectory-level scores, pairwise preferences, or chosen/rejected labels for complete outputs or full trajectories. These signals enter the Policy through RLHF/RLAIF (PPO, GRPO, REINFORCE), preference optimization (DPO, IPO, KTO), or filtered distillation (RAFT, BOND). This is the most densely populated and engineering-mature among the four cells, directly inheriting the classical RLHF pipeline. The themes below are cross-cutting narrative threads rather than mutually exclusive subcategories—a single work may be simultaneously online and preference-based, simultaneously BoN and filtered SFT.

**Classical RLHF and AI feedback replacing human feedback.** The classical RLHF paradigm—Christiano et al.'s preference-based policy optimization, Stiennon et al.'s summarization alignment, Ouyang et al.'s InstructGPT—established the prototype of P6 discriminative outcome: first train a parametric RM from preference data (P4), then use PPO to optimize the Policy against that RM; RM construction belongs to P4, this section focuses on the RM → Policy step. [Bai22b] replaced human feedback with AI feedback: in the RL-CAI phase, a constitution-guided AI judge outputs preferences on complete responses, a preference model is trained, then RLAIF writes harmlessness judgment into the policy (the SL-CAI phase's generative revision is covered in §6.1.2). [Lee23b] systematically compared RLHF and RLAIF, showing that an off-the-shelf LLM can either first provide preference data to train an RM (canonical RLAIF) or directly output outcome-level scores on complete responses to drive RL in d-RLAIF; its judge is a general-purpose LLM, belonging to the boundary cases noted above.

**Online and self-reflective evaluation.** [Guo24]'s OAIF has an online LLM annotator output preferences on complete response pairs sampled from the current policy, with preferences directly entering DPO/IPO/SLiC rather than being distilled into a separate RM, ensuring the supervision distribution is always on-policy. A special form is **self-reflective P6**—the source Evaluator and target Policy share the same set of parameters. [Yua24]'s Self-Rewarding Language Models has the same model serve as both generator and judge, self-scoring to construct winner-loser pairs and re-infusing them via iterative DPO. [Wu24d]'s Meta-Rewarding further adds a meta-judge to improve the judge's own scoring quality, forming an actor/judge/meta-judge co-evolution. In self-reflective P6, source and target carriers coincide, making it the form where P6 and P7 (knowledge externalization) are most tightly coupled (§6.1.5).

**Best-of-N distillation and filtered SFT.** This group does not use on-policy RL but instead uses the Evaluator to filter out high-scoring samples for supervised-style writing. [Don23]'s RAFT uses an RM to score multiple responses to the same prompt and retains the best-of-K for SFT. [Ses24]'s BOND distills inference-time Best-of-N selection into a single-sample policy, using forward/backward KL to approximate the BoN distribution defined by the RM. [Gui24]'s BoNBoN characterizes the best-of-n distribution offline and uses SFT-BoN and IPO-BoN to include both best and worst samples in training. [Gon24b] applies this approach to API agents: a critic LLM scores complete trajectories, and the top p% high-scoring trajectories serve as pseudo-demonstrations co-trained with general data through iterative SFT. In all these cases, the Evaluator's judgment is the load-bearing factor enabling data improvement, placing them in P6 under §6.1's criterion.

**Stability, debiasing, and multi-evaluator approaches.** When a policy repeatedly optimizes against a single imperfect Evaluator, reward hacking, length bias, and judge bias are amplified; this group targets those failure modes. [Ack26] interprets reward hacking as the policy exploiting sharp peaks in the proxy reward, adding gradient regularization and reference reset to stabilize outcome-level RL (its verifiable-reward sub-setting does not constitute clean P6). [She24c]'s policy filtration only admits the RM's most reliable high/low-scoring samples into the PPO buffer. [Wu25x] uses a Bayesian router to dynamically select among multiple trained RMs by query. [Xu24d]'s Mixture of Judges injects judgments from calibrated RMs, LLM judges, and rule-based constraint judges into CRPG/CODPO/CRRAFT (the rule-constraint portion is not clean P6). [Fis24] distills the RM's reward difference into the policy's implicit reward. [Ren26b] lets an LLM judge synthesize a scalar reward through the YES probability of meta-questions, updating the policy via GRPO/CISPO without dependence on ground-truth labels.

**Evaluators with process capability whose artifact falls at outcome.** Several works have Evaluators internally capable of step-level diagnosis, but what actually enters the Policy remains a trajectory-level scalar; under the "primary artifact driving update" criterion they belong here. [Din25b]'s FAPO uses a generative RM to locate the first invalid step and identify flawed-positive rollouts; this diagnosis is compressed into a penalty on the whole rollout before entering GRPO. [Che25v] uses a step-wise judge for post-hoc first-error identification on reasoning chains, converting it to a trajectory's partial-correctness scalar (Reasoning Trajectory Score), enabling even all-negative groups to produce gradients. [Li26p]'s OS-Themis internally has a milestone verifier and trajectory judge, but the online RL main signal is trajectory-level reward and the self-training branch filters successful trajectories through the Evaluator. These works exhibit a noteworthy phenomenon: the Evaluator's diagnostic granularity and the granularity of the artifact the Policy actually consumes can be decoupled.

Discriminative outcome's advantages are formal simplicity, strong compatibility with existing RLHF/DPO/rejection sampling frameworks, and easy scaling of scalar signals. The costs concentrate on two points: the Evaluator only assesses complete results and cannot tell the Policy which intermediate step caused success or failure, making credit assignment coarse for long-horizon tasks; when the Policy continuously optimizes against a fixed and imperfect Evaluator, reward hacking, length bias, sycophancy, and judge bias amplification emerge, and as the Policy drifts, an offline-trained RM gradually goes off-distribution and becomes distorted—the entire debiasing cluster above responds to precisely this structural fragility.

### §6.1.2 Generative Outcome Evaluator-to-Policy Transfer

Generative outcome evaluators produce explanatory, critical, or corrective natural-language content—critique, failure explanation, revised response—for complete outputs or full trajectories, and use this content (rather than a scalar) as the Policy's primary supervision.

An early instance is [Bai22b]'s SL-CAI phase: the feedback model generates a critique and revision of the complete response according to a constitution, and the Policy directly performs SFT on the revision. What enters training is a corrected complete response—granularity is outcome, form is generative—forming a contrast with the same work's RL-CAI phase discriminative outcome, illustrating that the same Evaluator's output can enter the same Policy through both discriminative and generative paths.

[Li26l]'s Co-Evolving Critics is a more recent and complete instance: a linguistic critic synchronized with the actor generates score-aware natural-language critiques on complete rollouts; the actor produces refined rollouts conditioned on these critiques; the Policy uses GRPO to optimize these refined trajectories; and the critic co-evolves on the same data. It treats "Evaluator co-updating with Policy" as the means to combat stale feedback, sharing the co-evolution idea with §6.1.1's self-reflective P6, but with generative critique rather than scalar as the feedback form.

This cell is the sparsest in the literature among the four, for structural reasons: the value of generative feedback lies in pointing out "where is wrong, why, and how to fix it," and this localization capability naturally gravitates toward step-level rather than whole-trajectory granularity—once an Evaluator is willing to produce fine-grained explanations, using them at process granularity yields higher information utilization than at outcome granularity. Generative evaluators cluster toward §6.1.4; purely outcome-granularity generative works are relatively rare.

Generative outcome's advantage is that the information content of a single signal far exceeds a scalar, providing the Policy with "why" supervision, and combining with co-evolution can mitigate Evaluator staleness. The costs are: critique and revision quality is capped by the Evaluator's own capability; hallucinated error diagnoses from the Evaluator will be absorbed by the Policy as correct supervision, while critique correctness itself is difficult to verify; trajectory-level correction still has coarse credit assignment for long-horizon tasks; revised trajectories are off-policy relative to the current Policy, introducing distribution mismatch; generative supervision is harder to scale than scalars.

### §6.1.3 Discriminative Process Evaluator-to-Policy Transfer

Discriminative process evaluators produce numerical or ordinal signals—step scores, turn-level advantage, token-level dense reward, step-wise preference—for intermediate steps, local actions, reasoning prefixes, tool-use steps, or state-action pairs, and transfer these step-level discriminative signals into the Policy. The core motivation is credit assignment in long-horizon agent learning: relying solely on terminal reward, the Policy cannot know which local behaviors to reinforce or avoid.

**Turn/step-wise value and critic.** [Zho25p]'s SWEET-RL trains a turn-level critic in multi-turn collaborative reasoning, using training-time privileged information to directly learn per-turn action advantage and distilling turn-wise credit assignment into the actor through DPO. [Wan26s] trains an Agentic-Q model in the GUI setting to estimate future return for state-action pairs, using step-wise value as local supervision during the SWPO phase. [Xu25h] targets non-verifiable multi-turn search, using a principle-based PRM to score each turn on correctness/relevance/coherence, calibrating through ReNorm with the terminal ORM before feeding into PPO + GAE.

**Dense reward unpacked from inside the Evaluator.** [Cha24b] does not construct additional process labels but instead extracts token attributions from the final-layer attention of a standard RM, redistributing the terminal scalar reward as token-level dense reward for PPO—the Evaluator is unchanged; what changes is the granularity at which reward is projected from the Evaluator to the Policy. [Wan26ac]'s CW-GRPO uses a rubric-based LLM judge to output two binary signals per search round (retrieval utility and reasoning correctness), synthesizing round-level contribution weights to redistribute trajectory-level advantage by round.

**Step-level filtering and behavior cloning.** [Zho24e]'s Language Feedback Model judges whether each action is productive; the Policy absorbs state-action pairs marked as desirable through behavior cloning. [Pan24b]'s evaluator gives success judgments on trajectories and performs per-step progress filtering in the device-control branch; its inference-time Reflexion component is not P6; only the Filtered BC branch—using Evaluator-filtered high-quality state-action pairs to update weights—is counted. In both cases, although the feedback could be extended to explanatory forms, what actually enters training is the yes/no productivity judgment and its filtering result, placing them in discriminative.

**Multi-dimensional rubrics and GUI step-level preferences.** [Din26e]'s AdaRubric first generates a rubric for the task, then outputs 1--5 scores per step per dimension, aggregating them for both trajectory-level DPO and step-level PPO; rationales primarily serve explanation rather than being the main training carrier, so it falls in discriminative, placed here because its core contribution is step-level multi-dimensional scoring. [Luo25f] uses a GPT-4o judge to score single-step GUI action candidates in the computer-use setting on a 0--100 scale, constructing step-level preferred/rejected pairs from high/low scores to train UI-TARS-2B.

Discriminative process's advantage is directly alleviating credit assignment, yielding denser gradients and higher sample efficiency in long-horizon and multi-turn tasks. The costs are heavier: reliable PRM training is expensive; process label noise is large and "what counts as a good step" is itself hard to define; PRMs themselves can be hacked—the Policy may learn to produce locally high-scoring but globally erroneous steps; calibrating step reward with outcome reward is difficult ([Xu25h]'s ReNorm, [Wan26ac]'s normalization are both responses to this); when step-level signals come from indirect sources such as attention attributions or privileged information, consistency with true credit lacks guarantees.

### §6.1.4 Generative Process Evaluator-to-Policy Transfer

Generative process evaluators produce explanatory, diagnostic, or corrective content for intermediate steps—step-level critique, first-error localization with refined step, revision trajectory, directive hint—and transfer this content into the Policy. Unlike discriminative process, which answers "is this step good or bad," generative process answers "why is this step bad and how should it be fixed." This cell is where the P6/P5/P7 boundary is most blurred: what enters the Policy is often a refined trajectory (a Tokenized carrier), and the corrective action is often generated by a strong Policy. Under §6.1's criterion, as long as the Evaluator's diagnosis is the load-bearing factor enabling the correction and the method's core is using the Evaluator to locate and correct experience, it belongs to P6; the P6/P7 entanglement is discussed in §6.1.5.

**Step-wise diagnosis and refinement.** [Zha26am]'s SRR-Judge outputs explanation, 1--5 rating, and refined thought/refined action for each thought-action step of a search agent; the rating handles filtering, while the refined step serves as the generative alignment target, written into the Policy through iterative RFT. [Hon25c]'s Natural Language Actor-Critic uses a generative language critic to produce natural-language critiques of future trajectories and quality judgments for each state-action pair; the actor generates refined actions conditioned on the critiques, which are then reverse-distilled back into the base policy—constituting an off-policy actor-critic in language space.

**Failure-driven reflection and correction internalization.** [Yua25c]'s Agent-R has the actor itself serve as the verifier, locating the transition point of bad trajectories in the search tree divergence, inserting a revision signal between the erroneous prefix and the correct continuation, and performing iterative SFT on "reflection plus revision" instances, teaching the model to actively recognize errors and switch to corrected action paths in multi-turn interaction. Agent-R enters the Policy through the text tokens of the revision trajectory, adjacent to P5; its P6 attribution rests on the self-assessment judgment (Good/Bad/Uncertain) being the load-bearing link enabling the construction of correction data, with the method centered on Evaluator-driven experience correction.

**Directive/hint-based correction.** [Wan26ad]'s OpenClaw-RL extracts directive textual hints from user utterances or environmental signals, using On-Policy Distillation to convert the log-prob gap between a hint-enhanced teacher and the student into token-level directional updates. The work's sequence-level binary RL component belongs to discriminative outcome; here we take its generative process main thread, with classification based on the paper's emphasis on "using language instructions to correct agent behavior."

Generative process has the highest information density and interpretability among the four cells, best suiting agent scenarios with complex error types and open task spaces, and is currently the most active frontier. The costs are also the heaviest: critique and refined step quality is capped by the Evaluator's capability ceiling; "who evaluates the Evaluator" is especially acute in generative form—an erroneous critique is more misleading than an erroneous scalar, and its correctness is difficult to automatically verify; refined trajectories are off-policy relative to the current Policy, with distillation facing distribution mismatch; generative step-level evaluation has high inference and training costs and is difficult to scale; the roles of Evaluator and teacher Policy heavily overlap in the refined-action branch, making P6 and P7 difficult to cleanly separate.

### §6.1.5 Discussion

**Uneven distribution across the four cells.** Discriminative outcome directly inherits classical RLHF, has stable engineering interfaces, and contains the most work. Discriminative process is rapidly expanding around credit assignment. Generative process is the most active frontier but most entangled with P5/P7. Generative outcome is the sparsest, for reasons discussed in §6.1.2—the localization value of generative feedback pushes Evaluators toward step-level granularity. The two axes are not symmetric: the granularity axis (outcome/process) answers "at what unit size does assessment fall," while the form axis (discriminative/generative) answers "in what form is assessment expressed," the latter simultaneously determining the trade-off between information density and verifiability.

**Evaluator exploitation is a cross-cell shared failure mode.** Whether the Evaluator outputs a scalar or a critique, at outcome or step level, as long as the Policy continuously optimizes against a fixed and imperfect Evaluator, it will converge toward the Evaluator's bias rather than the true objective. Three categories of response appear in the literature, each with a different emphasis: co-evolution keeps the Evaluator updated with the Policy ([Yua24], [Wu24d], [Li26l]); ensembling/routing uses multiple Evaluators to disperse single-point bias ([Wu25x], [Xu24d]); regularization and filtration constrain the optimization process ([Ack26], [She24c]). Under generative form this problem is more insidious—erroneous critiques are more misleading than erroneous scalars, and critique correctness lacks automatic verification.

**The tension of Evaluator as source carrier.** P6's treatment of Evaluator parameters as source carrier is a choice with inherent tension: the evaluated $(c, a)$ is the original source of the experience, while the Evaluator only carries the assessment facet of that experience. The P5/P6 boundary falls on the intermediary through which supervision passes—P5 is the direct writing of trajectories, demonstrations, or environmental signals; P6 must pass through the learned assessment capability of a parametric Evaluator. When the Evaluator is a trained RM/PRM, this distinction is clear; when it is an off-the-shelf judge whose assessment capability primarily comes from pretraining and is difficult to trace to concrete agent experience, §6.1 treats it as a boundary case.

**Self-reflective P6.** A recurring structure is the Evaluator and Policy sharing parameters ([Yua24], [Wu24d], [Yua25c]). Source and target carriers coincide, with P6 and P7 forming a closed loop within the same set of weights: the model externalizes assessments and then absorbs them. The sustainability of such work depends on whether assessment capability can improve in lockstep with generation capability; otherwise the iteration stagnates or amplifies its own biases.

**Trends and open problems.** The cross-cell trend is evaluators moving from discriminative to generative and from outcome to process—from answering "is it good" to answering "why is it not good and how should it be fixed." This trend raises the information density of supervision while also pushing the question of "how to guarantee the Evaluator's own reliability" to the foreground. Discriminative signals can at least be indirectly verified through held-out preference consistency; generative step-level critique correctness verification currently lacks mature methods, constituting the main open problem P6 leaves for subsequent work.

## §6.2 Parametric-to-Tokenized Transformation (P7)

P7 externalizes experience already internalized in parametric model weights into Token-level artifacts consumable by other systems. The source is Policy or Evaluator parameters (the models carrying these parameters can be implemented in any modality—LLM, VLM, VLA); the target is Tokenized carriers such as agent trajectories, tool-use demonstrations, GUI action traces, step-level labels, critiques, and preference annotations. P7 runs opposite in direction to P5 (Tokenized → Policy): P5 writes discrete experience into parameters; P7 reads reusable experience expressions out of parameters.

P7 remains a representation-to-representation pathway. The externalized trajectory is not created from nothing; it re-encodes the experience semantics once written into parameters via P4/P5, satisfying both Experience Grounding and Experience Embodiment (§2.3). What distinguishes P7 from P1–P6 is the form of the source. P3's source is a determinate trajectory, and the transformation operator is deterministic re-encoding. P7's source is parameters as an aggregated representation—the multiple original trajectories that contributed to it have been superimposed within the weight distribution and can no longer be individually localized; the transformation operator correspondingly contains a sampling step: $\mathcal{T}$ draws samples from $p(e \mid \text{prompt}, \text{env})$, rather than re-encoding a given $e$.

This source property directly determines two organizational facts about the entire P7 section. Sampling has variance; low-quality samples must be post-hoc culled by verifiers/filters, explaining why P7 methods almost without exception attach a verification component. Sampling can be repeated and the best samples retained, producing a best-of-N effect: a student trained on filtered teacher data can be more reliable than the teacher's single-sample average.

P7 must be distinguished from three adjacent processes. Ordinary inference where the model generates a CoT or answer serving only the current query, without independent archival and reuse intent, does not constitute P7. Self-training and iterative bootstrapping where the model's own generated rollouts are directly consumed by subsequent versions of the same training loop are closer to composite pipelines (§7). The distinction from P1/P2 reduces to one operable criterion: trace the transformation's input—if the input is an already-existing trajectory and the transformation is a re-expression of that trajectory, it belongs to P1/P2; if the input is only a prompt or task specification and the externalized content comes from the model's internalized capability, it belongs to P7.

By the downstream consumption role of the externalized experience, P7 divides into two categories: **Demonstration Externalization** externalizes "how to act" experience, sourced from Policy parameters; **Evaluative Supervision Externalization** externalizes "how to assess" experience, sourced from Evaluator parameters. The dividing line is where the Evaluator's judgment goes—if the judgment is materialized as independent, reusable supervision data, it falls in the latter; if it serves only as an internal filtering switch, it remains an internal quality-control mechanism of the former and does not constitute a separate class.

### §6.2.1 Demonstration Externalization

Demonstration Externalization refers to a parametric Policy or teacher agent externalizing its implicit decision-making capability into behavioral demonstrations for student imitation learning. Target artifacts are typically agent trajectories, tool-use dialogues, web/GUI/mobile interaction traces, or reasoning-action paths. Downstream consumers absorb these demonstrations through SFT, behavioral cloning, imitation-style training, or using them as initialization data for RL. Whether the consumption mode is SFT or RL does not change the artifact's carrier properties; classification follows the artifact's form, not how it is consumed.

The fundamental qualitative constraint of Demonstration Externalization is the capability ceiling: the student inherits the teacher's upper bound in task capability dimensions. P7 trades the teacher's one-time inference cost for the student's low inference cost, scalability, and domain specialization—not for capabilities exceeding the teacher. The best-of-N effect noted earlier can lift the student's reliability above the teacher's average level, but what is lifted is stability within the same capability frontier, not the frontier itself.

We use **degree of environmental synthesis** as the organizational axis for §6.2.1. It is anchored in the observation $o$ of the §2.1 quadruple: the more $o$ is an objective return from a real environment, the stronger the externalized trajectory's grounding; the more $o$ is generated by the model itself, the closer the trajectory is to a pure parameter readout. Along this axis, the risk of pseudo-success and hallucinated observation increases, and the necessity of a verifier rises correspondingly.

**Real-environment-grounded.** The teacher interacts on real websites, real MCP servers, or real operating systems; $o$ is an objective consequence returned by the environment. Explorer [Pah25] has GPT-4o autonomously discover tasks and execute multi-step interactions on real web pages in an exploration-driven manner, producing multimodal trajectories with page states, action sequences, and task summaries through a proposer/refiner/summarizer/verifier pipeline. InSTA [Tra25] pushes this to internet scale, having a strong model autonomously propose tasks, execute web operations, and generate reasoning traces on large-scale real websites, retaining only high-quality successful trajectories through judge-based filtering. AutoSurfer [Fai26] systematically surfs real websites to identify functional spaces, organizing them into task tuples containing page states, action histories, and reasoning. SynthAgent [Wan25ar] first performs element-level exploration and task synthesis on the target website, then globally refines after collection, producing synthetic supervision more closely fitted to the specific site distribution. Structured Distillation of Web Agent Capabilities [Lu26] uses an Agent-as-Annotators framework to have a Gemini teacher generate successful trajectories with structured reasoning blocks. On the computer and mobile side, Fara-7B [Awa25] produces screenshot-thought-action trajectories through FaraGen, filtered by a triple-verifier; OpenMobile [Che26d] has an expert take over to correct learner deviations, preserving corrected trajectories and step-level CoT—the corrective operations exposed at expert intervention points themselves become high-value supervision. TOUCAN [Xu25o] has multiple strong models synthesize tasks and execute complete tool-use rollouts in real MCP environments, with task-level and trajectory-level dual filtering forming a large-scale tool-agentic corpus; the multi-teacher design simultaneously mitigates distribution narrowing from a single teacher. ANCHOR [Wei26b] identifies branch points from a small number of high-quality seed trajectories, then has a strong model expand new tasks and trajectories around the branches—seeds only serve to locate expansion positions; the externalized new trajectories are still generated by teacher parameters.

**Simulated-environment synthesis.** The environment itself is played by models; $o$ is no longer a real return but the simulator's generated output. ToolAlpaca [Tan23] has ChatGPT separately play user, assistant, and tool executor, generating tool-use trajectories in {Instruction, Actions, Response} form through multi-agent simulation—an early work clearly demonstrating this paradigm. ToolMind [Yan25ab] generates multi-turn interaction trajectories with reasoning through three-agent (user/assistant/tool) simulation and performs fine-grained filtering at both trajectory and turn levels. ToolACE [Liu24o] first automatically expands an API pool, then synthesizes tool-call dialogues of varying complexity (single-step, parallel, dependent) through multi-agent self-guided dialogue. APIGen-MT [Pra25b] first generates task blueprints and ground-truth action sequences, then synthesizes multi-turn function-calling dialogues through simulated agent-human interplay, retaining trajectories consistent with the blueprint through rejection sampling. Learning with Challenges [Kan26b] first assesses the student's capability boundary, then has a multi-agent generator synthesize difficulty-adaptive mobile GUI demonstrations, making the externalized data fit the student's learning frontier. Under simulation, the fidelity of $o$ depends entirely on the simulator's own accuracy; the verifier's burden rises accordingly.

**Constructed synthesis (weak interaction).** This end has virtually no interactive environment; trajectories are constructed backward around pre-given answers, evidence, or question structures, closest to pure parameter readout. EviPath [Li25at] reasons backward from gold evidence to abductive reasoning paths, then has a strong model generate RAG trajectories containing planning, sub-question decomposition, and evidence-grounded answering. RAGShaper [Tao26] constructs information trees with distractors and multi-hop questions, inducing a strong teacher in a controlled retrieval environment to produce agentic RAG trajectories with thought, retrieval action, observation, and final answer. APIGen [Liu24n] has a strong model generate natural-language queries and structured function-calling answers based on real executable APIs, passing format/execution/semantic triple checks—it retains execution checks based on real APIs, but interaction depth is shallow, overall leaning toward generation rather than interaction, positioned between real-environment-grounded and constructed synthesis. At this end, the externalized trajectories have the highest consistency and are easiest to scale, at the maximum risk that $o$ diverges from the real environment.

The above methods all target Narrative trajectories, but Demonstration Externalization's target is not limited to Narrative. FABRIC [Ver25b] generates task descriptions, tool definitions, policy pseudocode, dialogue, and execution traces through an LLM-only pipeline; the policy pseudocode and task/tool schemas belong to Schematic Tokenized, constituting a **P7 → Schematic** sub-cell. Attribution here must use the criterion above: FABRIC's input is a prompt rather than an existing trajectory; the externalized program skeleton comes from the model's internalized capability, thus belonging to P7. Had a work performed formalized extraction on a batch of existing trajectories, it would belong to P2 rather than P7.

Demonstration Externalization's risks are distributed along the environmental synthesis axis. Teacher-generated trajectories may contain hallucinated actions, non-executable steps, or pseudo-success trajectories; this risk is significantly amplified at the simulated-environment and constructed-synthesis ends—when $o$ is generated by the model itself, a "successful" observation may merely be the teacher's self-confirmation. A single teacher also leads to distribution narrowing, making the student overfit the teacher's expression style and decision preferences; multi-teacher mixing (e.g., TOUCAN) is a common mitigation.

A separate class of work couples demonstration externalization with environment synthesis in the same pipeline. ASTRA [Tia26] constructs reinforcement arenas while synthesizing tool-use trajectories; AgentSynth [Xie25e] advances scalable task generation and benchmark construction while synthesizing computer-use trajectories; COVERT [Xu26e] constructs RL training environments while synthesizing verifiable tool-use instances; Mock Worlds [Lu26j] builds mock tool ecosystems while synthesizing demonstrations. In these works, the trajectory/instance portion is legitimate P7 and can be cited in the appropriate position; but the reinforcement arena, mock environment, and RL training environment are apparatus for generating future experience, not experience carriers—they fall outside the §2.2 carrier ontology and do not belong to P7, delegated to §7 under composite paper rules. Works that simultaneously use the model as UI simulator and teacher agent follow the same logic: the simulator role externalizes environment dynamics/world models, also outside the carrier system.

### §6.2.2 Evaluative Supervision Externalization

Evaluative Supervision Externalization refers to parametric Evaluators—judges, verifiers, critics, reward models—externalizing their implicit assessment capability into Token-level supervision for consumption by student models, agent systems, data filtering pipelines, or reward-model training processes. Unlike Demonstration Externalization, which externalizes "how to act," this class externalizes "how to judge action quality." The externalized source is still parameters (the Evaluator's judgment capability); the assessed trajectory is merely the substrate to which the judgment attaches. The P7/P1 criterion from §6.2 still holds, only with the source switched to Evaluator parameters.

Target artifacts include critiques, step-level labels, action correctness annotations, progress labels, preference pairs, verification traces, and failure diagnoses. By the granularity of the externalized signal, these methods divide into two branches.

**Step-level correctness annotation.** [He25g] first has a strong computer-use agent generate noisy trajectories, then has o4-mini grade each step on correctness and optimality, accompanied by reasoning-rich annotations including screenshot analysis, action review, and alternative analysis. The assessment results are materialized into WebSTAR and WebSCORE datasets—the former training student computer-use agents, the latter further distilling a lightweight StepRM. He25g simultaneously carries a Demonstration-side contribution (WebSTAR as student training data); we attribute it here to Evaluative based on its core artifact, with the demonstration side covered in §6.2.1. STEVE [Lu25e] uses GPT-4o as a step verifier, comparing interface states before and after action execution, generating binary correctness labels and verification reasoning for each step, thereby constructing step-verified trajectory data and training an open-source student agent via KTO.

**Long-horizon progress and process structure.** [Log26b] uses a judge to extract explicit constraints from web tasks, evaluating the constraint satisfaction state at each trajectory step to form fine-grained CSR progress signals, and performs prefix extraction and hindsight relabeling based on these signals, enabling even partially successful trajectories to be converted into trainable samples. Step-GUI [Yan25x] first establishes a quality anchor with a trajectory-level verifier, then has a strong multimodal thinking model transcribe the verification results into seven categories of structured step-level supervision—progress tracking, state summary, effect prediction, self-reflection, state verification, intent execution, action prediction—externalizing sparse trajectory-level success/failure judgments into dense process supervision.

Evaluative Supervision Externalization must be distinguished from P6. P6's core is that the Evaluator's reward/preference/critique directly participates in Policy optimization, causing Policy parameter updates. P7 emphasizes that the Evaluator's output is explicitly materialized as Tokenized artifacts and can be reused by other systems independently of the original Evaluator. This yields the unified dividing line between §6.2.1 and §6.2.2: many Demonstration Externalization methods use a judge to delete low-quality trajectories, but if the judge's output only serves as an internal filtering switch, it is merely a quality-control mechanism; only when the Evaluator's judgment itself becomes an independent, reusable artifact does it belong in §6.2.2. This line also demarcates the boundary between this section and several evaluative-but-coupled works—STeCa [Wan25x], CGI [Yan25s], ECHO [Li26l] do externalize critiques or calibrated supervision, but these artifacts primarily serve the same synchronously evolving training loop rather than being consumed as transferable data assets by subsequent systems, making them more appropriate as boundary cases between P7 and P6/composite.

### §6.2.3 Summary

Demonstration Externalization and Evaluative Supervision Externalization respectively externalize the Policy's decision-making capability and the Evaluator's assessment capability, corresponding to the two main threads of "how one should act" and "how to judge action quality." A single paper may involve multiple transformation pathways; placement depends on the type of artifact primarily consumed downstream—this is an observable mechanistic fact (did the downstream system take away a trajectory or a label?), consistent with §3.1's inclusion criterion of "judge by mechanism, not self-positioning." Many agent data synthesis pipelines simultaneously contain teacher rollout and evaluator filtering; classification still follows the core artifact.

At the framework level, P7 occupies a special position on the Experience Source attribute (§2.2.5): {teacher}-sourced experience almost entirely enters the corpus via P7; P7 is precisely the manufacturing process that produces the {teacher} experience source. This also distinguishes P7 from the other six pathways—P1–P6 process already-existing experience; P7 *produces* new experience carriers.

P7's core trade-off is determined by several interlocking factors. The capability ceiling limits P7 to not extending the teacher's capability frontier; what it exchanges is cost, inference efficiency, and domain specialization. Relative to human annotation, P7 trades the teacher's one-time inference cost for supervision data at nearly arbitrary scale, at the cost of data quality doubly constrained by teacher capability and sampling variance. The verifier plays a dual role in this trade-off—both absorbing sampling variance and, through the best-of-N effect, lifting the filtered student's reliability above the teacher's average level. The degree of environmental synthesis determines $o$'s grounding strength and the verifier's burden. Lateral design dimensions—environment-mediated synthesis, multi-teacher mixing, curriculum and difficulty-aware sampling, execution-based verification, step-level filtering, human-in-the-loop screening—all act upon the quality, scale, diversity, and verifiability of the externalized artifacts.
