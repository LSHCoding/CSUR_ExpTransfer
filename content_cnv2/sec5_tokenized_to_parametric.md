# §5 Tokenized-to-Parametric Transformations (P4 + P5)

This chapter discusses two pathways through which experience moves from Tokenized carriers into Parametric carriers: P4 (Tokenized → Evaluator, solidifying experience as assessment capability) and P5 (Tokenized → Policy, solidifying experience as decision-making capability). Both pathways share the same source type; the difference lies in the functional role of the target carrier—Evaluator parameters answer "how to judge action quality," while Policy parameters answer "how to generate actions."

## §5.1 Tokenized-to-Evaluator Transformation (P4)

P4 internalizes Tokenized agent experience into parametric Evaluators. Source experience includes agent trajectories, reasoning traces, tool-use logs, action histories, preference pairs, execution outcomes, and failure annotations as discrete records. Through training, the evaluative semantics within these records are written into Evaluator parameters, forming reusable assessment capability. Trained Evaluators manifest as outcome reward models, process reward models (PRMs), verifiers, critics, judges, value models, failure detectors, or diagnostic feedback models.

The hard criterion for P4 inclusion is that **Evaluator parameters must be substantively updated by experience data**. Prompted LLM-as-a-judge approaches, which only activate the existing model's assessment capability at inference time without parameter updates, do not qualify. The criterion concerns only whether parameters are updated, without restricting the training method—SFT, RL, self-training, and contrastive learning all serve as valid internalization approaches; this section indeed contains all of them.

This section organizes P4 work along two orthogonal axes.

The **primary axis is supervision granularity**, distinguishing the level at which training-time supervision signals are allocated. **Outcome-supervised** methods allocate signals at the level of complete outputs, full trajectories, or entire episodes—whether the task was completed, whether the final answer was correct, which of two complete responses was better. **Process-supervised** methods allocate signals at the level of intermediate steps, action prefixes, or individual decisions—whether a particular reasoning step, tool call, GUI action, or state-action pair is correct or shows progress. Granularity is determined by where the supervision signal is attached, not by how many steps the task has: a multi-step agent task where the Evaluator only receives a label at the terminal outcome is still outcome-supervised. The two subclasses are mutually exclusive and constitute §5.1.1 and §5.1.2.

The **secondary axis is artifact form**, distinguishing what the trained Evaluator outputs at inference. **Discriminative** evaluators produce only scalars or signals directly readable as scalars—single scores, binary correctness, pairwise preference probabilities, reward vectors. **Generative** evaluators produce natural language or structured generative content—critiques, error diagnoses, failure explanations, verification rationales, repair suggestions, structured justifications. This axis avoids a literal scalar/non-scalar dichotomy: a substantial body of work produces artifacts with both scalar and generative components (first generating a rationale, then a verdict), for which a literal dichotomy would leave them unclassifiable. Between discriminative and generative forms there exists a unidirectional information-projection relationship—a generative evaluator's output can always collapse to a scalar (verdict tokens read as probabilities), while the reverse does not hold. A work is attributed to the generative subclass whenever its method contribution includes generative output, following its richer component. The secondary axis cuts once within each of §5.1.1 and §5.1.2.

A small number of Evaluators carry both outcome and process supervision. Such works are attributed by their **dominant supervision signal** to the corresponding subsection, with secondary components noted in the text; no separate hybrid category is created—outcome and process in these works represent differences in degree rather than a categorical opposition, and forcing them into parallel categories would weaken the primary axis's discriminative power.

### §5.1.1 Outcome-Supervised Evaluator Internalization

Outcome-supervised evaluator internalization trains Evaluators to give global judgments on complete candidate behaviors, mapping a complete response, trajectory, or episode to an outcome-level assessment. Its appeal is that outcome-level labels are generally easier to obtain than dense process labels and can directly align with the final objective for tasks that have definite final answers or explicit success criteria. Its structural limitation is weak credit assignment: a failed trajectory may contain many valuable intermediate decisions, and a successful trajectory may contain incidentally effective but non-robust reasoning; compressing an entire trajectory into a single label obscures which actions should be reinforced and which should be corrected.

#### §5.1.1.1 Discriminative Outcome Evaluators

Discriminative outcome evaluators produce a scalar score or discrete label for a complete candidate, with the shortest downstream consumption path.

The most concentrated cluster is episode-level success/failure detectors in the embodied domain. Vision-Language Models as Success Detectors [Du23] reformulates success detection as visual question answering, using human-annotated clip-level success labels to fine-tune Flamingo's vision-relevant layers via SFT, learning to answer yes/no for (video clip + task description) pairs. I-FailSense [Liu25ac] targets cross-task failure detection through two-stage training—first LoRA SFT on a VLM, then training FailSense blocks attached to intermediate layers, using cross-entropy and BCE to internalize success/failure signals from expert demonstrations and mismatched-negative pairs. SAFE [Zha25ad] extends the input from pure observations to the VLA policy's internal latent embeddings, using an MLP with L1 objective or an LSTM with BCE objective to learn trajectory-level failure prediction; its training label is only the coarse-grained signal of whole-trajectory success/failure (outcome), yet at inference it can output failure likelihood at each timestep, triggering abort or human intervention via conformal thresholding. RoboReward [Lee26b] pools OXE demonstrations and RoboArena rollouts with human-verified progress scores into experience, constructs 1--5 level episode rubrics through counterfactual relabeling and temporal clipping, and trains Qwen3-VL's fusion and LLM layers via SFT with MAE regression on discrete episodic rewards.

Discriminative outcome also covers trajectory-level preference models and web-domain evaluators. VLP [Liu25w] does not rely on human preferences, instead taking expert/medium/random manipulation video segments from Meta-World, using three types of implicit preference rules (Intra-Task, Inter-Language, Inter-Video) to construct comparison labels, and training a trajectory-segment-level cross-modal preference model via cross-entropy; the product is a single scalar preference score, used as reward annotation for offline RL. WebRL [Qi24] trains an outcome-supervised reward model within a self-evolving online curriculum RL framework, using WebArena-Lite web rollouts and an online-generated trajectory buffer to judge whether a complete web trajectory satisfies the task, based on user instruction, action history, and final page state. WebRL also learns a value network critic—but this critic is updated by rollout returns and GAE coupled to the current policy, invalidated as the policy iterates, functioning as a training scaffold for policy optimization rather than an independently reusable Evaluator artifact; the truly stable P4 product, reusable across episodes, is the outcome RM. POLICYGUARD [Che25ae] compiles WebArena trajectories with domain metadata into POLICYGUARDBENCH, performing full-parameter SFT on Qwen3-4B to train policy violation detection as a strictly formatted binary instruction-following task; it judges both complete trajectories and prefixes of varying lengths—this is the same violation detector applied to different input lengths, not two different supervision granularities, so it remains outcome.

#### §5.1.1.2 Generative Outcome Evaluators

Generative outcome evaluators produce semantic content beyond a verdict for complete candidates—judging rationales, failure reasoning, repair suggestions—yielding higher information density at the cost of greater inference and output overhead.

The main thread in the reasoning and general domain is generative reward models and verifiers. Generative Reward Models [Mah24b] reformulates reward modeling as next-token generation, using UltraFeedback and UltraInteract preference pairs with self-generated rationales, training direct SFT GenRM, CoT-GenRM with reasoning chains, and STaR-bootstrapped SFT/DPO variants—the direct variant only exposes a verdict token externally (readable as scalar), while the CoT variant outputs rationale plus verdict. Generative Verifiers [Zha25o] likewise treats reward modeling as next-token prediction, using SFT to learn Yes/No verdicts and a unified loss to jointly learn solution generation and verification; GenRM-CoT first produces a verification reasoning chain then a verdict, used downstream for Best-of-N reranking and weighted self-consistency. Both works sit at the discriminative/generative boundary: their direct variants do produce only a verdict collapsible to scalar; they are attributed to generative because the method contribution is inseparable from the CoT variant, and the next-token form itself allows the output to carry verification reasoning. S2J [Sun25l] identifies a solving--judging capability gap in generative reward models, requiring the Evaluator to first solve the problem itself before judging, using a joint RL objective (DAPO/GRPO) with both solving reward and judging reward to update parameters. J4R [Xu25i] uses EIS-GRPO to train a pairwise judge, driving RL with judgment correctness over equivalent initial state subgroups (via response order swap etc.), suppressing position bias and surface format interference. Critic-RM [Che25aq] has the model self-generate critiques as latent rationales, jointly optimizing critique generation loss and Bradley-Terry preference loss; the artifact carries both continuous reward and explanatory critique. AgentV-RL [Li25ay] makes the verifier a multi-turn tool-augmented verification agent—first rejection fine-tuning to learn high-quality verifier trajectories, then GRPO with the consistency between final verdict and ground truth as reward; the artifact is a multi-turn verification process containing code execution and a True/False conclusion.

Another group of generative outcome evaluators takes "helping downstream repair" as the training objective. CTRL [Xie25c] targets code, composing problems, candidate programs, critiques, and post-repair program performance into experience, with hidden unit test pass/fail as reward—first SFT using execution-guided synthetic critiques, then GRPO directly optimizing critiques to better help the generator produce test-passing code; the artifact is analysis, actionable suggestions, and correctness judgment. RL4F [Aky23] trains a critique generator with PPO reward derived from the refined output's task metric relative to ground truth; the artifact is feedback text, sent back to a black-box task model for output repair. ECHO [Li26l] uses dual-track GRPO to co-evolve critic and policy—the critic generates high-level diagnostic critiques on complete trajectories, with reward coming from the performance gain of the critique-guided refined trajectory over baseline; the training signal is thus attached to the refined trajectory's final performance, making it outcome.

The embodied and computer-use domains have also produced generative outcome evaluators. CriticGPT [Don25e] takes manipulation videos from policies at different Meta-World training stages, uses environment full-information scripts to automatically generate pairwise analysis and preferences, and LoRA fine-tunes LLaVA-1.5 to learn visual comparison and textual analysis; the core Evaluator directly updated by experience is CriticGPT itself, and the downstream scalar reward model distilled from it belongs to the utilization stage. ARMOR [Qi26] trains a robotic failure detector with large-scale sparse failure labels and smaller-scale dense reasoning annotations, jointly optimizing a BCE detection head and a next-token reasoning decoder, absorbing its own rollouts in an online refinement phase; the artifact carries both binary failure labels and natural-language causal analysis. Video-Based Reward Modeling for Computer-Use Agents [Son26d] constructs ExeVR-53k, performs reward modeling fine-tuning on Qwen3-VL and introduces spatiotemporal token pruning for long videos, jointly learning holistic success judgment and first-failure attribution—the main task is video-level success (outcome), while first-failure attribution provides structured localization in temporal intervals, making the artifact exceed pure scalars.

**§5.1.1 Discussion.** Within the outcome subclass, discriminative and generative evaluators present different utilization profiles. Discriminative outcome evaluators (success detectors, episodic RMs, trajectory preference models) have the shortest downstream consumption path and lowest latency, suiting reward-filtered behavior cloning, offline RL annotation, and runtime guardrails. Generative outcome evaluators trade higher inference and output overhead for interpretability and correctability: their verdicts can still collapse to scalars for P6, while the rationale/critique component supports usage modes like critique-driven refinement—these usages often manifest within a single paper as the chained structure "critique drives one round of revision, then the revised trajectory is used for training," treated as composite in §7.

### §5.1.2 Process-Supervised Evaluator Internalization

Process-supervised evaluator internalization trains Evaluators to provide local supervision on intermediate reasoning steps, action prefixes, or individual decisions. The core motivation is that agent failure is typically not a sudden event at the final step but the accumulation of a series of local errors; process evaluators attempt to answer the question that outcome supervision flattens—not only whether the final outcome is good, but whether each intermediate decision is correct, promising, and advancing task progress.

#### §5.1.2.1 Discriminative Process Evaluators

Discriminative process evaluators output scalar scores for each step, prefix, or state-action pair, forming the largest cluster within this branch.

The reasoning PRM lineage established the fundamental paradigm of local supervision and has continuously refined its semantics. Let's Verify Step by Step [Lig23] uses PRM800K's human step annotations to train a PRM, giving positive/negative/neutral judgments per step; the artifact is per-step scalar correctness probability. OmegaPRM [Luo24] uses MCTS plus binary search to automatically locate the first error, converting prefix completability into soft process labels to replace human annotation, and comparing soft pointwise, hard pointwise, and pairwise Bradley-Terry objectives. Rewarding Progress [Set24] shifts the supervision target from step correctness to step-level advantage—estimating future success probability from Monte Carlo rollouts starting at each prefix, discretizing into buckets to train a Process Advantage Verifier that assesses whether a step genuinely improves future success probability. PQM [Li24m] changes PRM training from independent classification to comparative Q-value ranking with margin, requiring correct prefixes to have higher success probability than incorrect ones and a significant value break at error points. Error Typing [Pal25] introduces hierarchical supervision, with two-stage token prediction first predicting math error and consistency error types, then the final correctness reward. BiRM [Che25s] jointly learns backward-looking process reward and forward-looking value heads, simultaneously encoding "whether the current step is reasonable" and "whether this prefix still has hope for the future."

The agentic, GUI, and web domains ground these abstract signals into step-level evaluation directly aligned with environmental state changes. AgentPRM [Xi25b] extends reasoning PRM to multi-step agent decisions, using TD and GAE to automatically construct step-wise Q-values and advantage pseudo-labels from rollouts, jointly learning promise and progress heads via MSE. ProgRM [Zha25z] uses an LCS-based recipe self-annotation algorithm to locate key steps in GUI trajectories and assign normalized progress labels, training a Progress Reward Model via BCE to predict the current action's progress position within the task recipe after execution. Building Autonomous GUI Navigation via Agentic-Q Estimation [Wan26s] back-propagates the terminal success signal to state-thought-action triples, using BCE to train a step-wise success probability estimator, then performs policy optimization via SWPO combined with GRPO/RLOO/REINFORCE++. Advancing Mobile GUI Agents [Dai25] employs Pairwise Process Preference—at each step, the correct action scores higher than several incorrect actions—fine-tuning the backbone and scoring head via Q-LoRA. IntentScore [Che26s] incorporates planning intent as conditional information into the action encoder, using contrastive alignment and margin ranking to learn single-step evaluation of (interface state + candidate action intent), serving test-time reranking with extremely low latency. GAIA [Wan26p] iteratively trains a step-level binary critic through a data flywheel—SFT for the first-round critic, then letting it mine harder samples to train subsequent rounds, continuously incorporating the real error distribution of GUI agents. In the code domain, SWE-Shepherd [Dih26] assigns heuristic immediate rewards to intermediate actions (reading files, editing, running tests), discount-accumulates them into step-wise targets, and trains a PRM via qLoRA with MSE.

Several embodied visual reward models likewise fall within discriminative process. RL-VLM-F [Wan24l] has GPT-4V/Gemini first produce analysis then pairwise labels on observation image pairs, using Bradley-Terry-style preference learning to learn a continuous reward function; supervision is attached to states or short segments rather than a complete episode verdict. LIV [Ma23d] combines VIP-style temporal value pretraining with CLIP-style InfoNCE, imposing learning signals on the relative positions among initial, intermediate, and goal frames, forming a dense reward without action annotations. Video-Language Critic [Ala24] learns through contrastive symmetric cross-entropy plus a sequential ranking objective, forcing scores of later segments in success videos to be higher than earlier ones, outputting scalar rewards for video prefixes; its contrastive objective targets full-video-to-instruction matching with an outcome component, but the artifact is per-timestep and the supervision carries explicit local temporal ordering constraints, placing it in process.

#### §5.1.2.2 Generative Process Evaluators

Generative process evaluators produce critiques, error diagnoses, or corrective suggestions for each intermediate step—not only judging whether a step is good or bad, but also explaining what constraint was violated, what type of error occurred, and in what direction correction should proceed.

In the reasoning domain, generative PRMs have deepened step-level critique. DeepCritic [Yan25u] estimates post-step success probability via Monte Carlo and locates the first error, with two phases of critique teaching (SFT) and critique incentivization (GRPO); the artifact is long-form deliberate rationale, per-step positive/negative judgment, and a first-error index. Enhancing LLM Reasoning via Critique Models [Che25ar] constructs MathCritique-76k—controlled-generation flawed reasoning paths on GSM8K and MATH plus GPT-4o critiques of the first erroneous step, soft-filtered to retain samples truly helping correction—training the critique model via SFT to output step-level labels and constructive feedback, simultaneously serving test-time conditional refinement and training-time exploration data rewriting. StepWiser [Luo25h] takes intermediate reasoning chunks from NuminaMath-CoT and their Monte Carlo rollouts, labeling them positive/negative by the Q-value change before and after, using GRPO to directly reinforce the judge's generation of correct stepwise verdicts and explanations, making it learn meta-reasoning.

The GUI and web domains contain the densest concentration of generative process evaluators. GUI-Shepherd [Che25h] pools AndroidWorld online rollouts and AndroidControl offline states into 52k interaction experiences, with human positive/negative labels per step action and GPT-4o supplementary explanatory rationales, performing SFT on UI-TARS-1.5-7B to learn step-level verification, and studying a CoT verifier variant that jointly learns reasoning and judgment within parameters; the artifact can be positive/negative logits or rationale plus judgment. GUI-Critic-R1 [Wan25q] is a pre-operative critic—before action execution, it generates observation analysis, possible result, critique, 0--1 correctness score, and corrective suggestion—first RFT cold-start, then Suggestion-aware GRPO reinforcing correctness, reasoning, and suggestion quality. OS-Oracle [Wu25m] integrates seven open-source GUI trajectory datasets into 310k step-level samples, simulates four common error types as negatives through rules, first SFT to learn reasoning plus judgment, then CP-GRPO with accuracy, format, and reasoning-consistency rewards to reinforce a cross-platform critic. WebArbiter [Zha26ab] is a principle-guided generative PRM—using WEBPRM COLLECTION of approximately 30k step-level preference pairs, first reasoning distillation with o3 teacher reasoning, then GRPO aligning the final verdict with ground-truth correctness; the artifact contains principle induction, structured justification, and preference verdict. Web-Shepherd [Cha25b] frames web process evaluation as checklist-grounded generative judging—using next-token prediction, the model, given instruction, checklist, and state-action pair, generates feedback text and Yes/No/In-Progress judgments for each checklist item, convertible to scalar reward through a verbalizer. Don't Act Blindly [Che25as] trains an action-effect verifier—negatives constructed by fabricating pseudo-histories where the post-action interface shows no change—first robust SFT, then Verification-Action-Effect GRPO with a composite reward of action quality, effect honesty, and verification accuracy; the artifact is reasoning with Verify/Diagnose/Recall labels and a SUCCESS/NO_CHANGE verdict.

The embodied domain's generative process evaluators combine per-timestep progress judgment with textual analysis. AHA [Dua24] programmatically generates failed subtasks from RLBench success demonstrations through FailGen, performing autoregressive next-token training under the LLaVA instruction-tuning paradigm, enabling the model to simultaneously output yes/no judgments and free-text failure rationales for intermediate subtasks in long-horizon manipulation, usable for reward rewriting, TAMP planning correction, and zero-shot subtask verification. SOLE-R1 [Sch26] converts real and simulated robot videos into 1.2M spatiotemporal CoT traces, automatically obtaining per-timestep progress supervision through geometric distance, temporal reversal, and deviation injection; first SFT to learn structured reasoning plus progress values, then RLVR and GRPO to optimize progress judgment accuracy; the artifact is spatiotemporal analysis text in the think region plus an integer progress score in the answer region. Large Reward Models [Wu26e] uses LoRA, SFT, and DPO on the same Qwen3-VL backbone to learn contrastive reward, absolute progress reward, and completion reward respectively, internalizing the temporal experience of 24 video sources into a unified LRM—progress and contrastive being process supervision, completion being outcome supervision; the artifact is reasoning-first analysis text plus reward label. In the general agentic setting, PPRM [Xu25h] targets non-verifiable multi-turn search tasks, having GPT-4o and Qwen3-235B-A22B score step-wise by principles of correctness, relevance, and consistency, training PPRM via SFT to produce analysis text and normalized scores; at policy optimization, ReNorm mixes process reward with outcome reward.

**§5.1.2 Discussion.** The process subclass exhibits a clear evolutionary trajectory: early work first established the necessity of step-level evaluators in reasoning, then extended local supervision from correctness to advantage, Q-value, progress, and bidirectional reward semantics, and subsequently grounded these abstract signals in GUI, computer-use, code, and embodied domains as Evaluators directly aligned with environmental state changes and task progress. The inherent cost of process evaluators is obtaining step-level supervision—human step annotation is expensive, and automated schemes (MCTS rollout, binary search for first-error localization, TD/GAE pseudo-labels, LCS recipe matching) essentially trade computation for annotation, with the reliability of these pseudo-labels directly capping the Evaluator's upper bound.

Between discriminative and generative process evaluators, the generative branch has the highest information density, suiting scenarios with complex error types and open task spaces, and also aiding cross-task transfer—diagnoses like "unverified tool return," "ignored user constraint," or "prematurely stopped search" may recur across different tasks. Its cost is reliability: natural-language critiques may sound plausible yet be inconsistent with real environmental feedback, and refinement suggestions may mislead downstream agents; in high-stakes tasks, generative process evaluators typically need to be combined with environmental verification, program execution, or human audit.

A monotonic relationship exists between artifact form and downstream utilization freedom—generative artifacts have the highest information content, can be collapsed to scalars for P6 RL, taken as binary for Best-of-N filtering, or retained in full text for step-wise refinement, yielding the greatest utilization freedom; discriminative artifacts face the most constrained downstream options, consumable only as numerical signals. This relationship is further developed in §8 and in the connection with P6 (Evaluator-to-Policy).

## §5.2 Tokenized-to-Policy Transformation (P5)

P5 internalizes discretized agent experience into Policy/Actor parameters. Source experience takes the form of various Tokenized artifacts—natural-language trajectories, tool invocation records, code editing histories, GUI operation sequences, reasoning chains. The goal is for this experience to enter Policy weights, enabling the model to perform sequential decision-making in subsequent tasks without explicitly retrieving, concatenating, or replaying the original experience.

The question P5 answers is not how to train a stronger model, but rather how the discrete experience formed during agent interaction is organized into training signals that rewrite the Policy's parametric decision-making capability. Unlike P1 or P2, where the target carrier remains readable text, rules, programs, or workflows, P5's target carrier is unreadable, non-locally-addressable weights. Once experience is internalized into parameters, invocation cost is low and context-window constraints are eliminated, at the cost of reduced explicit traceability and local modifiability.

We classify P5 into three categories along a single axis: which facet of a trajectory's experience the policy update consumes. **Imitation-based Policy Internalization** consumes the action sequences of successful trajectories themselves, having the model directly imitate. **Environment-Reward-based Policy Internalization** consumes the objective outcome of each trajectory, optimizing with verifiable environmental feedback. **Preference-based Policy Internalization** consumes the relative ordering between trajectories, constructing preferences and optimizing.

The P5/P6 boundary is determined by the source of the reward or preference signal. When the signal is non-parametric—environmentally verifiable results, unit tests, ground-truth matches, rule-based verifiers—experience directly follows Tokenized → Policy (P5). When the signal comes from a parametric evaluator (trained RM, PRM, LLM-as-a-judge), the transformation first passes through Tokenized → Evaluator (P4), then Evaluator → Policy (P6), constituting a composite path and falling outside this section. When a parametric evaluator is used only for filtering or reconstructing training data and its scores do not enter the policy update loss, experience is still directly internalized and the work still belongs to P5.

### §5.2.1 Imitation-based Policy Internalization

Imitation-based Policy Internalization treats high-quality agent trajectories as supervised demonstrations, training the Policy through behavior cloning, SFT, or rejection-sampled fine-tuning. It consumes the action sequences of successful trajectories themselves: if a set of trajectories already embodies effective task-solving behavior, the model can internalize the corresponding experience into parameters by imitating its action selection, reasoning patterns, tool-use habits, or GUI operation sequences. Environmental feedback here does not enter the optimization objective but only serves data selection and data construction—filtering out failed rollouts, invalid tool calls, and retaining code modifications that pass tests.

The mainline mechanism is success-filtered behavior cloning, where environmental signals determine which data enters the training set and the loss remains maximum likelihood on successful data. A web agent work filters self-generated trajectories in WebArena by environment error and self-critique, performing autoregressive SFT under QLoRA [Pat24]. SWE-Gym uses executable unit tests to judge patch trajectory success/failure and completes policy updates through rejection-sampling fine-tuning; the verifier trained in the same paper only does reranking and does not alter the agent's classification [Pan24]. Go-Browse collects web trajectories through structured exploration and performs SFT only on successful ones, with success labels given by a VLM-as-a-judge but used solely for filtering [Gan25b].

A class of work performs lightweight processing on trajectories before imitation to improve the quality and coverage of demonstration signals. NNetNav [Mur24b] performs hindsight instruction relabeling on unsupervised exploration trajectories in open web pages, rewriting undirected interactions into instruction-following demonstrations before SFT. AndroidGen [Lai25d] uses StepCritic to segment long Android trajectories into successful sub-segments and applies augmentation, then performs LoRA-SFT on the filtered trajectories. These processing steps only rearrange or relabel trajectory content without changing the carrier level and remain preprocessing within P5. When processing reconstructs experience across carriers, it forms a chained composite with P1 or P2, attributed to §7: WebCoT verbalizes reflection, branching, and rollback traces into explicit CoT before internalization [Hu25i]; AgentTrek compiles web tutorials into a Goal-Observation-Reasoning-Action schema before SFT [Xu24].

Several works bear names like RFT, ReST, or RL while the actual policy update remains maximum likelihood on success-filtered data. AppVLM is termed ReST or RFT, but failed trajectories receive zero return and do not enter the training set; the core update is success-only SFT [Pap25b]. A VLA work's two-stage framework uses binary reward on the action head for exploration in Stage 1, then full-model MSE supervised learning in Stage 2—reward's role is data discovery, while the full-model's actual update mechanism is imitation [Guo25d]. P5 subclass attribution is determined by the objective used when policy weights are rewritten, not by the paper's method naming.

Imitation-based approaches suit agent cold-start, rapidly teaching the model action space, output protocols, and basic interaction workflows. AgentTuning further co-trains agent trajectories with general instruction data to preserve general capabilities [Zen23d]. The capability ceiling is constrained by demonstration coverage—when training data only contains success paths from limited scenarios, the model easily learns surface formatting or local heuristics and struggles to actively explore new strategies.

### §5.2.2 Environment-Reward-based Policy Internalization

Environment-Reward-based Policy Internalization has the agent interact with the environment to produce trajectories and directly optimizes the Policy using non-parametric, verifiable feedback signals. It consumes the objective outcome of each trajectory: task success/failure, unit test pass/fail, code execution feedback, SQL execution correctness, ground-truth answer match, GUI task completion status, manipulation success/failure—all programmatically determinable by the environment itself. The "Environment-Reward" label serves to demarcate the boundary: works where the reward comes from a trained RM or LLM-as-a-judge belong to P6. Unlike Imitation, which only learns what successful trajectories did, here failed trajectories, converted to negative signals through reward, also enter optimization.

The basic premise was established by early work. GLAM [Car23] places FLAN-T5 in BabyAI-Text and uses task success sparse reward via PPO for online grounding. LLaRP [Szo23] connects a frozen LLaMA with a visual encoder as an embodied policy, using environmental rewards composed of success, subgoal completion, and invalid-action penalty, training adapters and heads via DD-PPO. As long as the agent's actions can be executed in the environment and the environment can return objective verifiable results, Tokenized trajectories can be directly converted into Policy weights. Under these short-horizon or single-objective settings, sparse reward with PPO sufficed.

When settings advance to long-horizon, multi-turn real environments, this premise comes under pressure. WebAgent-R1 [Wei25] uses M-GRPO to sample browser trajectories in parallel, with rewards given by URL match and Playwright verification. A long-context SWE work [Gol25] uses DAPO, with reward from validation test pass/fail plus length penalty. These push RL into real multi-turn environments while simultaneously exposing the core difficulty—a trajectory of dozens of steps receives only a single sparse verifiable reward at the terminal outcome, and this signal is difficult to utilize effectively. Making a sparse yet verifiable reward usable in long-horizon multi-turn settings is the central problem of subsequent work; it advances along three layers—keeping the signal from vanishing, making it attributable, and making it dense at the source.

The first layer is keeping the signal from vanishing. Long-horizon agents easily collapse to low-exploration strategies early in training; once collapsed, the policy no longer produces successful trajectories, and under sparse reward no positive samples can be learned. One group treats entropy as a controllable quantity to combat collapse: EPO [Xu25k] adds trajectory-level entropy regularization and entropy smoothing atop PPO or GRPO, alleviating the exploration-exploitation cascade failure in sparse-reward multi-turn RL. Agentic Reinforced Policy Optimization [Don25d] uses entropy-based adaptive rollout in multi-turn tool-use, triggering local branch sampling at high-uncertainty steps and assigning different advantages to shared-prefix and branched actions. AEPO [Don25c] performs entropy-balanced clipping and dynamic rollout budget allocation. EMPG [Wan25ad] scales per-step advantage by current entropy and adds a future-clarity bonus encouraging entry into low-entropy successor states. Another group directly supplements positive samples when signals are already scarce: a GUI work maintains a successful trajectory replay buffer and injects positive samples from the historical buffer when the current sampling group has entirely failed and no gradient is available [Lu25f]. SoLS [Pap25c] performs off-policy asymmetric updates—strong updates for positive-advantage samples and conservative regularization for negative-advantage samples—combined with successful transition replay. AgentGym-RL [Xi25c] uses ScalingInter-RL with a short-to-long horizon curriculum, letting the agent first master basic interactions on completable short tasks before progressively lengthening. UI-S1 [Lu25j] follows a semi-online approach, rolling out with the model's self-generated history and having a patch module continue when the rollout deviates from the expert.

Once signals can be continuously produced, the second layer is making them attributable. In multi-turn trajectories, reward often arrives only at the terminal outcome, while the decisive factor may be a single intermediate step; accurately allocating a sparse return to decision units is the core of this layer. A clear evolutionary line is the continuous sinking of attribution granularity: from whole-trajectory, to turn-level, to step-level. ArCHer [Zho24f] uses environmental reward to first train an utterance-level Q critic, then transmits multi-turn advantage to the token actor. Turn-PPO [Li25ae] models multi-turn interaction as a turn-level MDP and uses a critic for GAE at turn granularity. GiGPO [Fen25c] uses anchor-state grouping to compare the actions of different rollouts at the same intermediate state, simultaneously constructing episode-level and step-level relative advantage. IGPO [Wan25y] uses the increment in ground-truth answer log-prob before and after each interaction round to define an information-gain intrinsic reward, converting the true information contribution of observations into a turn-level signal. Parallel to granularity sinking is a branch that avoids the variance and overhead of learned critics: GiGPO's group-relative estimation and LOOP's leave-one-out PPO both replace explicit value networks with value-free approaches [Fen25c, Che25af]; Q-SFT [Hon24] uses the Bellman target of offline data as sample weights for token cross-entropy, formal in appearance as SFT but mechanism as value-based credit assignment. The critic in actor-critic setups belongs to V-Par, raising a classification question: does this already fall into P6? The critic is bootstrapped from environmental reward within the RL algorithm, an internal auxiliary for estimating expected return, not an independently trained external reward source; the reward's provenance remains the environment. ArCHer and Turn-PPO thus remain in P5, differing fundamentally from P6 where the evaluator serves as the reward source.

The third layer reaches closer to the root: rather than struggling harder with exploration or attribution on a fixed sparse reward, make the reward signal itself denser at the source. The approach is to mine programmatically verifiable intermediate structures from the environment, rewriting a single terminal flag into a composite reward. ToolRL [Qia25b] decomposes reward into format correctness and three-level matching of tool name, argument name, and argument value. SQL-Trail [Hua26d] uses six components—execution, turn-budget, schema grounding, syntax, format—to form a composite reward within a reason-execute-observe loop. SQL-ASTRA [Li26r] uses Column-Set Matching Reward to derive dense partial credit from comparing result table column-value sets. RLVMR [Zha25ao] provides rule-verifiable process rewards for meta-reasoning behaviors like planning, explore, reflection, and monitor. In GUI and VLA domains, rule-based verifiable rewards are directly used as GRPO inputs—format legality combined with action-type, click-point, text-match, and other determinable items [Luo25b, Zha25an, Li25aa]. The more complex the environment, the more the truly high-value reward lies not in that terminal flag but in those programmatically verifiable intermediate structures, progressively mined and rewritten into the optimization objective. Credit assignment makes a fixed sparse signal more accurately attributable; reward construction makes the signal denser at the source. The center of gravity in recent years is moving from the former toward the latter.

A separate branch focuses on scaling this training regime: AgentRL [Zha25ag] uses fully asynchronous rollout and task advantage normalization to support multi-task mixed training; [Gu26b] performs asynchronous online RL at scale on mobile emulators and examines generalization across instance, template, and app levels. These concerns lean toward systems and benchmarks and are orthogonal to signal usability.

Environment-Reward-based approaches have a higher capability ceiling than Imitation—the Policy can discover new strategies beyond demonstration data through trial and error—at the cost of reward sparsity, credit assignment difficulty, expensive online rollouts, and reward hacking. It shares with Imitation the property that experience, once written into weights, is non-locally-addressable, and adds a further layer of irreversibility: RL updates are driven by high-variance gradients, and capability damage from a single collapse or reward hacking instance cannot be rolled back like editing a text rule. In practice, imitation and reward are often two stages of the same pipeline—cold-start with imitation to establish format, capability breakthrough delegated to reward optimization; the SWE work noted above first RFT warm-starts on test-passing trajectories before DAPO [Gol25], and SQL-Trail and AgentCPM-GUI both do SFT warm-up before RL [Hua26d, Zha25an].

### §5.2.3 Preference-based Policy Internalization

Preference-based Policy Internalization constructs relative preference relations between trajectories from agent experience and updates the Policy through DPO, DMPO, or other trajectory-pair optimization methods. It consumes the relative ordering between trajectories: relatively better trajectories come from successful tasks, verified executions, corrected trajectories, or more efficient operation paths; relatively worse ones come from failed attempts, erroneous tool calls, or code modifications that did not pass tests. The motivation is direct—in many agent settings, precisely designing per-step rewards is difficult, but comparing which of two trajectories is better is relatively easy.

Relative to the other two categories, Preference-based explicitly consumes failure experience: Imitation discards failed trajectories, Environment-Reward compresses them into a negative scalar, while Preference pairs failed trajectories as negatives with successful or corrected ones, letting the Policy learn behaviors to avoid. DMPO [Shi24c] extends DPO from single-turn response preference to multi-turn trajectory preference, introducing state-action occupancy measure and length normalization to address the preference objective under long trajectories. ETO [Son24] first performs behavior cloning with expert trajectories, then lets the Policy actively explore, pairing successful demonstrations with exploration-produced failed trajectories into preference pairs for continuous DPO updates. Agent-RLVR [Da25] targets SWE agent reward sparsity, using guidance to help the model explore repair trajectories more likely to succeed, then constructing preference pairs based on unit test pass/fail—its core objective is DPO, preference labels come from non-parametric unit tests, placing it in Preference-based. A tool-use work mines failure branches from the inference tree, pairing key steps on successful paths with same-level failed sibling nodes as step-level preferences [Che24k].

Preference granularity is likewise sinking, parallel to the credit assignment evolution in §5.2.2. HPL [Gao25c] notes that trajectory-level comparison alone is too coarse for long-horizon tasks, jointly optimizing trajectory-level, group-level, and step-level preferences, with groups partitioned by fixed, entropy, or semantic segmentation. WEPO [Liu25z] shrinks preference to the web element level, using contrastive training of target elements against distractor elements to improve local action discrimination. Environment-Reward and Preference, two separate routes, have independently moved toward finer attribution or contrast granularity.

Compared to Environment-Reward, Preference-based uses supervised-style training without needing to directly estimate high-variance policy gradients, yielding more stable training. Compared to Imitation, it can use negative samples and trajectory contrast, better suiting the handling of failure, correction, and exploration experience. Its limitations are that preference pair construction quality directly determines effectiveness, and trajectory-level preference may still lack fine-grained credit assignment. It shares with the other two categories the property of writing experience into weights within P5 and the same post-internalization non-local-addressability. Relative to Environment-Reward, its training process is closer to SFT without the sharp collapses from high-variance RL gradients, at the cost that expressiveness is bounded by pairwise comparison—"how much better" one trajectory is cannot be encoded, only "better."

**Classification criteria for the three categories.** The axis of §5.2 determines subclass attribution: when the core objective is SFT, behavior cloning, or imitation, with environment feedback used only for filtering or constructing samples, it is Imitation-based; when the core training signal is non-parametric environmental feedback, execution feedback, rule-based verifier, or ground truth, and it directly enters policy optimization, it is Environment-Reward-based; when the core objective is preference or trajectory-pair optimization with preference labels from non-parametric signals, it is Preference-based. When reward or preference primarily comes from a parametric evaluator such as a trained reward model or LLM-as-a-judge, the work belongs to Evaluator-mediated Policy Optimization (P6).
