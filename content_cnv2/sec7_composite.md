# §7 Composite Experience Transformation Pipelines

Sections §3–§6 discussed 7 single pathways, each characterizing a one-shot transformation between a single pair of carriers. The LLM-based agent literature also contains a class of more complex methods: they organize multiple transformation steps into chained or closed-loop pipelines, with experience flowing continuously among Narrative Tokenized, Schematic Tokenized, Policy parameters, and Evaluator parameters, and where the integration mechanism between adjacent steps itself constitutes the method's primary contribution.

Whether a work qualifies as Composite depends not on whether it contains multiple operational steps, but on whether a substantive integration mechanism exists between those steps. A paper that first generates agent trajectories and then trains a policy with them, where the intermediate steps are merely routine data collection and routine fine-tuning, is more appropriately attributed to single-step P5. A paper belongs to Composite only when its core contribution lies in *how* intermediate experience is filtered, repaired, calibrated, relabeled, verified, or scheduled in a closed loop so that it can be effectively consumed by a downstream policy or evaluator.

This chapter classifies Composite Pipelines into three categories. The three categories are demarcated by a unified criterion axis—**which carrier node the integration mechanism acts upon**:

- **§7.1 Evaluator–Policy Co-Evolution**: the integration mechanism is a learned evaluator and policy *mutually calibrating* each other. The criterion is that evaluator parameters are deliberately designed to be continuously refreshed or retrained alongside the evolving policy, with the two shaping each other within a shared closed loop. A critic's routine joint update in standard actor-critic does not constitute this class—only when "evaluator-policy co-evolution" is itself a contributed design element does it qualify.
- **§7.2 Refinement-Mediated Policy Internalization**: the integration mechanism acts upon the *content of individual experience instances*. The criterion is performing reflection, repair, calibration, purification, posterior writing, or abstraction on individual trajectories, altering their content as supervision signals, then internalizing the refined artifact into Policy parameters.
- **§7.3 Generative Experience Curation**: the integration mechanism acts upon the *generation and distribution of experience collections*. The criterion is producing experience that did not previously exist through exploration, synthesis, or search, constructing its distribution through verification, filtering, or selection, and using the curated experience set for Policy updates.

The one-sentence boundary between §7.2 and §7.3: **§7.2 rewrites the content of experience; §7.3 constructs the set of experience**. A work containing both operations is placed by its headline contribution—STeCa's core is step-level calibration (rewriting deviation segments of individual trajectories), placing it in §7.2; He25g's core is letting only high-quality steps contribute to the loss after step-level grading (not rewriting content, only deciding inclusion/exclusion), placing it in §7.3.

Orthogonal to the criterion axis is **composition topology**: chain (experience flows unidirectionally along directions like Narrative → Schematic → Parametric) vs. loop (downstream products flow back upstream, iterating over multiple rounds). Topology is not used as a primary classification axis but as an attribute descriptor for each pattern—§7.1 is almost entirely loops, §7.2 almost entirely chains, §7.3 contains both.

Two scope boundaries must be stated here. First, all composite works in this chapter have intermediate and terminal carriers falling on Tokenized and Parametric; we have found no typical representative of composite pipelines with Latent (KV cache, soft prompts, continuous memory tokens) as intermediate or terminal carriers—this may be either a genuine gap or a reflection of an immature direction. Second, the vast majority of works in this chapter take Policy parameter internalization as the endpoint; composites whose terminal is "a better Tokenized artifact without parameter updates" only partially appear in boundary cases like Sar24b in §7.2, constituting a position yet to be filled.

Compared to single pathways, Composite methods generally possess stronger experience utilization capability—raw agent trajectories contain a mixture of successful strategies, failed actions, locally correct steps, environmental feedback, error recovery, and noise. Composite pipelines organize these signals into more reliable training, assessment, or improvement mechanisms through multi-stage transformation. The cost is greater susceptibility to integration error, error feedback accumulation, distribution drift, and closed-loop collapse. Each category concludes with a typology of its integration mechanisms and corresponding failure modes; §7.4 closes with unified evaluation criteria spanning all three categories.

## §7.1 Evaluator–Policy Co-Evolution

### Definition and Criterion

Evaluator–Policy Co-Evolution refers to the composite pathway in which Evaluator parameters and Policy parameters mutually calibrate and co-evolve around agent experience. Its abstract structure can be written as a closed loop mediated by Tokenized waypoints: the Policy rolls out in the environment, producing new behavioral distributions and interaction experience (Tokenized); this experience is used to update or calibrate the Evaluator's assessment capability; the Evaluator in turn shapes Policy updates through reward, critique, preference, verification, or process feedback. It should be noted that Policy and Evaluator do not engage in direct parameter-to-parameter transformation—experience is first externalized as Tokenized trajectories, then separately drives parameter updates at both ends; the loop's actual structure contains a Tokenized waypoint.

The criterion falls on "whether evaluator-policy co-evolution is a contributed integration mechanism." In open-world, GUI, and embodied settings, the Policy's behavior continuously drifts with training; a static Evaluator easily becomes stale—it may fail to recognize new failure modes or produce erroneous assessments of new strategies. If an old Evaluator continues supervising a new Policy, the Policy may overfit the Evaluator's biases, even achieving high scores through reward hacking without genuinely improving capability. All works included in this section treat this problem as a named design objective and provide concrete mechanisms for keeping the Evaluator evolving alongside the Policy. Works that merely jointly update a critic in the standard actor-critic sense, with the contribution lying in *how the critic is used* (e.g., EVPO [Pan26], whose core is adaptively gating critic usability by explained variance), do not belong here and are closer to single-pathway RL critic usage details.

### Representative Works

**Core group—bidirectionally refreshed evaluator closed loops.** UI-Genie [Xia25e] places a GUI agent and reward model in a multi-round self-improving loop: the agent's exploration trajectories are filtered for high-value behaviors through reward-guided beam search, while failed trajectories are automatically converted into new step-level supervision through continuation rollout to refresh the reward model; improved evaluator precision directly raises subsequent trajectory filtering quality, and the stronger agent in turn produces richer experience fed to the next round's reward model. MagicGUI-RMS [Li26n] constructs a hierarchical assessment system with DS-RM and GP-RM, using automated feedback reflux to place policy and evaluator in the same closed loop—high-quality actions endorsed by GP-RM flow back as agent supervision, while divergence samples between DS-RM and GP-RM flow back as incremental training data for the evaluator. ECHO [Li26l] directly identifies critic staleness as the core obstacle in open-world agent learning: the policy generates on-policy trajectories, the critic provides multi-perspective natural-language diagnoses, and the policy performs conditional refinement; both undergo dual-track GRPO updates on "initial trajectory–diagnosis–refined trajectory," with saturation-aware gain shaping maintaining the critic's sensitivity to fine-grained improvements in high-score regions.

**Extensions in reasoning and embodied settings.** RL Tango [Zha25y] alternately trains a generator and verifier in the reasoning setting, keeping the verifier continuously updated on the generator's current reasoning trajectory distribution—the verifier receives only outcome-level correctness reward yet learns to produce step-level verification feedback that shapes the generator, while the generator's new trajectories continuously expose new verification challenges. RLAnything [Wan26u] extends the evaluator-policy coupling to a "policy–reward–environment" ternary loop: the reward model is continuously updated by the evolving policy's trajectories, its step-wise feedback jointly driving policy optimization with the outcome signal, while introducing theory-motivated environment adaptation to dynamically adjust task difficulty.

**Weak coupling form.** VARP [Sin25b] in embodied RL has reward learning explicitly depend on the current policy's rollout data through agent-regularized preferences, causing the reward model to re-estimate preference boundaries as policy capability changes. Its coupling strength is weaker than the core group—the evaluator is not retrained by a dedicated refresh mechanism but bound to the current behavioral distribution through a regularization term—yet assessment capability does evolve with the policy, placing it in this section as the weak end of the coupling spectrum.

**Limit case—internalized evaluator.** Self-Guide [Wan26aj] has a single language agent first generate a short self-guidance at each step, with this signal simultaneously serving as inference-time action steering and training-time internal reward. Here the evaluator is internalized within the policy itself; no independent second Parametric carrier exists, and the closed-loop structure $\mathcal{C}^P_\pi \leftrightarrow \mathcal{C}^P_\phi$ degenerates to a self-rewarding circuit on a single carrier. Its co-evolution logic still holds (a stronger policy produces more reliable guidance, and denser guidance in turn improves the policy), so it is retained in this section but annotated as the limit case of evaluator-policy parameter sharing, reminding the reader that the standard dual-carrier framework does not apply here.

### Integration Mechanism Typology and Failure Modes

| Integration Mechanism | Function | Representative Works |
|---|---|---|
| Alternating update | Generator/policy and verifier/evaluator optimized alternately | RL Tango |
| Feedback reflux | High-quality actions flow back as supervision; divergence samples flow back as retraining data | MagicGUI-RMS |
| Continuation rollout / hard negative mining | Mining potentially correct steps from failed trajectories to refresh evaluator | UI-Genie |
| Critic refresh + dual-track optimization | Critic synchronized with policy; both jointly optimized on same batch of experience | ECHO |
| Policy-conditioned evaluator training | Reward learning explicitly depends on current rollout distribution | VARP, RLAnything |
| Internalized self-guidance | Evaluator function generated by policy itself | Self-Guide |

The shared objective of these mechanisms is preventing Evaluator–Policy decoupling. Their primary risk is co-adaptation bias: if the Evaluator's training data primarily comes from the current Policy, it inherits the Policy's exploration blind spots and behavioral biases, and the two may jointly converge to a local optimum that is ineffective for external tasks. When evaluating such methods, one should examine whether the closed loop introduces external verification, hard negatives, independent evaluator refresh, or explicit anti-collapse mechanisms—closed loops lacking these components often produce high scores from mutual accommodation between evaluator and policy rather than genuine capability improvement.

## §7.2 Refinement-Mediated Policy Internalization

### Definition and Criterion

Refinement-Mediated Policy Internalization refers to the composite pathway that first transforms raw agent experience into a more learnable refined artifact, then internalizes it into Policy parameters. The typical structure is Narrative Tokenized → Narrative/Schematic Tokenized → Policy parameters. Source experience is typically raw trajectories, failure episodes, reasoning traces, tool-use logs, GUI interaction traces, or embodied execution records.

The criterion is that the integration mechanism acts upon *the content of individual experience instances*—performing reflection, repair, calibration, purification, posterior writing, or abstraction on individual trajectories, reinterpreting their content as supervision signals. Its key assumption is that agent historical experience is not naturally equivalent to learnable supervision: failure trajectories may contain correct prefixes and locally effective actions; successful trajectories may also contain redundant actions or non-transferable shortcuts. The distinction from single-step P1 is that the refined artifact enters training rather than remaining at inference-time context. The distinction from direct P5 is that it rejects the assumption that "raw trajectories themselves are reliable supervision," requiring experience to be rewritten before being written into parameters. The distinction from §7.3 is the object of operation—this section rewrites the internal content of individual experiences; §7.3 constructs the distribution of experience collections.

### Representative Works

**Reflection, repair, and rewriting of failure experience.** [Ge26] extracts rollback targets and reflective summaries from failed rollouts, generates corrected branches based on them, and through counterfactual distillation enables the model to reproduce the corrected decision without explicitly depending on the reflection text, internalizing explicit reflective agency as parametric capability. Agent-R [Yua25c] constructs revision trajectories with reflection signals from MCTS-generated good and bad trajectories, teaching the model to recognize the first critical error and switch to the corrected path in time. STeCa [Wan25x] refines the repair granularity to the step level: locating the first action deviating from the expert path, generating a calibrated trajectory with reflective thought and corrected action replacing the original deviation segment. CLEANER [Xu26j] targets noise contamination in tool calls and code execution, using similarity-aware adaptive rollback to purify trajectories containing error context into self-purified trajectories, with logit recomputation to suppress distribution shift. AgentHER [Din26] does not discard failure trajectories but extracts their actually achieved outcomes, generates hindsight goals in reverse, rewrites failed runs as successful demonstrations consistent with the real effects, then uses relabeled pairs for SFT or DPO.

**Posterior writing and reconstruction of process structure.** Watch Every Step! [Xio24] uses Monte Carlo rollout to push sparse outcome reward down to concrete steps, constructing step-level contrastive pairs and trajectory-level preference pairs, and writing the local quality structure of the process level into the policy through joint optimization of step-DPO, outcome-DPO, and SFT. ReAct Meets ActRe [Yan24m] has the agent explore to obtain action sequences, then uses ActRe to reverse-write posterior reasoning for these actions, reconstructing originally unexplained action traces into trainable ReAct-style trajectories, then internalizing through contrastive self-training. GUI-Reflection [Wu25b] automatically constructs GUI self-reflection data containing reflection thought, rollback, and correction through offline error fabrication and online failure trajectory mining, internalizing self-inspection and self-repair behavior in GUI scenarios as end-to-end policy. The commonality among these three works is reinterpreting raw action traces lacking trainable structure as trajectories carrying process supervision signals.

**Skill/hint abstraction followed by distillation.** Skill-SD [Wan26al] first summarizes multi-turn agent trajectories as structured natural-language skills (success analysis, mistake analysis, golden workflow), then uses these skills only as privileged information for the training-phase teacher, absorbing them into a student policy that does not explicitly access the skills through self-distillation. Online Experiential Learning [Ye26f] incrementally refines online interaction trajectories into experiential knowledge, compressing the "knowledge-carrying teacher" into a "knowledge-free student" through on-policy context distillation, with controlled experiments showing that extracting experience before internalizing outperforms directly training on raw trajectories. Memento No More [Ala25] uses corrective hints as intermediaries: a reviewer identifies error patterns from trajectories and generates targeted hints; a teacher produces a better behavioral distribution guided by the hints; the student learns to reproduce these behaviors without receiving the hints through context distillation.

**Boundary cases.** RetroAgent [Zha26] post-hoc converts raw trajectories into two types of refined feedback—intrinsic numerical reward for RL updates and actionable textual lessons. Its reward branch belongs to this section's "refine then internalize," while the textual-lessons branch is closer to inference-time memory (single-pathway P1); the method as a whole spans this section and evaluator-shaped policy learning and is treated as a boundary case. Sar24b refines noisy embodied trajectories into embodied programs of thought containing causal explanations, state changes, and subgoal structures, simultaneously used for retrieval and SFT; its SFT branch belongs to this section, while memory-writing and inference-time reuse are equally central—it precisely signals a class of "Tokenized-terminal composite": experience, after refinement, remains in Tokenized carriers for reuse without parameter updates—a path type almost entirely absent from the rest of this chapter.

### Integration Mechanism Typology and Failure Modes

| Integration Mechanism | Target of Action | Representative Works |
|---|---|---|
| First-error localization | Locating the first critical deviation action in a trajectory | Agent-R, STeCa |
| Counterfactual branch construction | Generating correction branches from failure point without reflection text | Ge26 |
| Hindsight relabeling | Reverse-rewriting failure trajectories' goals by actual outcome | AgentHER |
| Similarity-aware purification | Removing erroneous context, purifying trajectories | CLEANER |
| Posterior reasoning synthesis | Reverse-writing reasoning for action traces | ReAct + ActRe |
| Step-level process decomposition | Pushing outcome signals down to steps | Watch Every Step! |
| Skill/hint abstraction + privileged-info distillation | Abstracting into structured skills/hints then distilling | Skill-SD, Online Experiential Learning, Memento No More |

The advantage of this class is fuller utilization of failure and process experience—traditional imitation learning favors successful demonstrations, while this section's methods can extract error patterns, counterfactual corrections, and improved behaviors from failure trajectories. Their failure modes concentrate on the fragility of the refinement step itself: mislocating failure causes, interpreting contingent factors as general patterns, over-reliance on a strong teacher model, and loss of control over refined artifact abstraction level (too shallow and it is indistinguishable from raw; too deep and executable detail is lost). Evaluation should test whether the refinement step has been ablated—if the controlled experiment of removing the refinement step and directly training on raw trajectories is absent, the core hypothesis that "experience needs to be rewritten" remains unverified.

## §7.3 Generative Experience Curation

### Definition and Criterion

Generative Experience Curation refers to the composite pathway whose core integration mechanism is the generation and distributional construction of experience. Its typical structure is Policy/Teacher parameters → Tokenized → Policy parameters: Policy or teacher model first externalizes trajectories, demonstrations, interaction traces, or synthetic tasks; these Tokenized experiences are subsequently verified, filtered, selected, searched, or relabeled, constructed into training data approximating the target distribution; finally used to improve Policy parameters.

The criterion is that the integration mechanism acts upon *the generation and distribution of experience collections* rather than the content of individual experiences. This section's methods do not rewrite the internal content of individual trajectories; they decide which experiences are produced, which are retained, and how the overall distribution is composed. The core assumption is that limited interaction experience is insufficient to support reliable policy learning—experience needs to be actively expanded (exploration, synthesis, search) and quality-controlled (verification, filtering, scoring); what truly determines method effectiveness is how these intermediate steps turn raw agent experience into learning signals that are larger in scale, higher in quality, and better distributed. This section previously existed in an earlier version as negatively defined "Other Composite," but the internal works share a consistent integration logic, sufficient to be positively named as an independent pattern.

### Representative Works

**Exploration and synthesis-driven experience generation.** OS-Genesis [Sun24b] does not first assign tasks and then sample trajectories; instead, it performs interaction-driven traversal in the GUI environment, collects action-centered traces, reverse-synthesizes high-level tasks from them, and uses a trajectory reward model for stratified sampling of complete trajectories. AgentTrek [Xu24] transcribes web tutorials into structured task specifications, executes guided replay in real environments to generate multimodal trajectories, and verifies instruction adherence and goal completion through a VLM evaluator—tutorial text does not directly train the policy but is first organized into an executable task scaffold. AutoSurfer [Fai26] systematically explores websites with breadth-first coverage, obtaining more comprehensive action traces, synthesizing grounded tasks from them, and using the same exploration traces as hints to guide trajectory refinement. OpenWebVoyager [He24e] first obtains an initial web policy through imitation learning, then lets the agent continuously self-explore on real web pages; a fixed external judge (GPT-4o) performs trajectory-level rejection sampling on rollouts, and only judged-good trajectories flow back into subsequent training (its judge is not retrained with the policy, so this does not constitute §7.1 co-evolution). Bootstrapping Language-Guided Navigation [Wan24ad] constructs a generator–navigator self-refining flywheel: an instruction generator produces instructions from unlabeled trajectories, a navigator filters high-fidelity samples by execution consistency metrics (SPL, nDTW), and these samples in turn improve the generator—this is a rare loop topology in this section, but what flows back is experience quality rather than evaluator parameters. BLAZER [Das25] uses a strong LLM to zero-shot generate manipulation demonstrations in a simulator, with environmental execution verifying success/failure, and only automatically verified demonstrations retained for fine-tuning a smaller manipulation agent.

**Verification and filtering as core experience curation.** Scalable Data Synthesis with Step-Level Filtering [He25g] notes that even globally successful CUA trajectories contain numerous local errors or suboptimal actions, so it performs step-level grading on every action, letting only high-quality steps contribute to the supervision loss while preserving erroneous steps as context. Policy Improvement using Language Feedback Models [Zho24e] first distills a Language Feedback Model from large-model feedback, then uses it to judge the desirability of local actions in rollouts, screening out behavioral segments more worth imitating, and finally absorbing through imitation learning—its LFM is a learned but post-training fixed filter; the method's essence is evaluator-mediated filtering. DigiRL [Bai24] performs autonomous rollouts in real device environments, uses a fixed VLM-based AutoEval to provide reward signals, then performs instruction-level curriculum selection and step-level doubly-robust filtering, updating the policy with advantage-weighted regression. WebRL [Qi24] generates new curriculum tasks from the failure set, filters tasks through critic-based difficulty filtering and feasibility filtering, then after agent rollout selects usable experience through outcome reward model and actor-confidence replay filtering. WebRL jointly updates actor and critic, but the critic's joint update is standard RL plumbing; its contributed integration mechanism is the self-evolving data pipeline composed of task generation and experience selection, placing it in this section rather than §7.1. PLD [Xia25h] first trains a residual actor to probe the base VLA's failure regions, then collects successful trajectories containing both base-policy-like deployment distribution and recovery behavior through hybrid rollout, and finally distills back into the generalist VLA—its emphasis is not on residual RL but on how the three stages of probe, curate, and distill convert failure-targeted exploration into distribution-aligned training data.

**Search-structured experience reorganization.** MCTS-EP [Xu25q] uses MCTS to expand the trajectory space in an embodied environment, not only extracting successful paths but also constructing preference pairs at branch nodes from the long-horizon values obtained during search, used separately for SFT and DPO—search here reorganizes raw interaction experience into two trainable carriers: success trajectories and structured preferences. Agent Q [Put24] generates search trees and multi-branch trajectories through MCTS, combines critic ranking with outcome reward to form node-level preference signals, and compiles them into DPO training data, further compiling search experience from an exploration process into supervised preference artifacts. Trial and Error [Son24] has the agent actively explore and explicitly collect failure trajectories, then converts failure–success pairs into the contrastive training data required by DPO, replacing high-variance online RL—it does not rewrite the content of individual trajectories, but constructs contrastive supervision at the collection level through pairing, placing it in this section rather than §7.2.

### Integration Mechanism Typology and Failure Modes

| Integration Mechanism | Function | Representative Works |
|---|---|---|
| Interaction-driven traversal | Exploring environment and collecting traces without task guidance | OS-Genesis, AutoSurfer |
| Reverse task synthesis | Reverse-synthesizing high-level tasks from traces | OS-Genesis, AgentTrek |
| Simulator generation + execution verification | Zero-shot simulator generation + execution verification retaining successes | BLAZER |
| Search-tree expansion → preference compilation | Compiling search trees into node-level preferences | MCTS-EP, Agent Q |
| Learned / metric-based filtering | Filtering experience using learned evaluators or execution metrics | Zho24e, Bootstrapping LGN |
| Step-level grading | Scoring at sub-trajectory granularity then selecting supervision steps | He25g, DigiRL |
| Rejection sampling | Fixed judge performing trajectory-level acceptance/rejection on rollouts | OpenWebVoyager |
| Curriculum generation | Generating new tasks from failure set and filtering by difficulty | WebRL |
| Contrastive pairing | Pairing success/failure trajectories to construct preference data | Trial and Error |

This section's methods treat experience as intermediate material requiring secondary organization, verification, and compilation, rather than directly replayable raw trajectories. Their failure modes concentrate in three areas. The first is distribution drift: generated experience deviates from the target deployment distribution; PLD's emphasis on distribution-aligned data directly targets this. The second is verifier/filter unreliability: the biases of a fixed judge or reward model are amplified by the filtering mechanism, contaminating the entire training set. The third is insufficient exploration coverage and closed-loop mode collapse: a self-refining flywheel lacking diversity constraints converges to a narrow experience subspace. Evaluation should test whether the generation–filtering mechanism has been ablated, whether the verifier's reliability has been independently verified, and whether semantic mismatch between synthetic tasks and real tasks has been detected.

## §7.4 Chapter Summary

Composite Experience Transformation Pipelines extend experience reuse from single-carrier transformation to chained or closed-loop processes spanning carriers, modules, and stages. The three composite categories correspond to three integration logics: Evaluator–Policy Co-Evolution addresses the dynamic matching of assessment and decision-making capabilities, with the question being how to let the Evaluator co-evolve with the Policy while avoiding co-adaptation bias; Refinement-Mediated Policy Internalization addresses the enhancement of experience learnability, with the question being how to rewrite mixed raw experience into more stable training signals through reflection, repair, calibration, purification, or abstraction; Generative Experience Curation addresses the active expansion and organization of experience space, with the question being how to construct more reasonably distributed training data from limited interaction experience through generation, exploration, search, and filtering. The three categories' boundaries are given by the unified criterion axis—whether the integration mechanism acts on a pair of parametric carriers (§7.1), the content of individual experience (§7.2), or the distribution of experience collections (§7.3).

The potential benefit of Composite methods is the ability to utilize failure experience, process feedback, synthetic experience, search results, and evaluator signals—experience sources that single pathways struggle to independently cover. The cost is that each intermediate step may introduce new noise, bias, or misattribution, and errors can accumulate and amplify along the composite chain. When evaluating a Composite pipeline, four aspects should be examined: whether the integration mechanism is necessary (whether a controlled experiment removing that step exists), whether it has been ablated, whether it possesses mechanisms to prevent error accumulation (external verification, anti-collapse, independent refresh), and whether it genuinely improves the agent's generalization capability on new tasks and new environments.

This chapter also reveals two gaps in the literature distribution that can serve as indicators for subsequent research: composites with Latent carriers as intermediate or terminal are almost nonexistent, yet §4 has shown that single-step Tokenized → Latent transformation is a mature direction—an unexplored combinatorial space exists between the two; composites whose terminal is "a better Tokenized artifact without parameter updates" are similarly sparse, with boundary works like Sar24b that simultaneously serve retrieval and training hinting that such memory-terminal composite pathways merit examination as an independent form.
