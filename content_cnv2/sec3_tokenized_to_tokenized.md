# §3 Tokenized-to-Tokenized Transformations (P1 + P2)

This chapter discusses two transformation pathways that keep experience within the Tokenized carrier level: P1 (Narrative → Narrative) and P2 (Narrative → Schematic). Both share the same source—concrete trajectories produced during agent sequential decision-making (task execution logs, reasoning traces, tool-use trajectories, web navigation histories, GUI interaction traces, or embodied execution records)—and differ in the formalization degree of the target carrier and its downstream consumption mechanism.

Tokenized-to-Tokenized transformations are distinct from Parametric-to-Tokenized externalization (P7): the former requires concrete agent trajectories, execution logs, or interaction records as input, with the model serving as abstractor, formalizer, or synthesizer; if the artifact is primarily generated by the model from task descriptions or internalized knowledge without concrete trajectories as input, it belongs to P7.

A note on terminology and classification. The literature exhibits severe inconsistency in naming similar objects: "skill" can refer to executable code, natural-language step descriptions, or hierarchical knowledge base entries across different papers; "memory" and "experience" likewise suffer from both homonymy (same name, different mechanisms) and synonymy (different names, same mechanism). Our classification does not rely on papers' self-reported terminology. Instead, it rests on two observable properties: the structural form of the artifact (natural language text, parseable/executable structure, or graph), and the downstream consumption mechanism (read and understood by an LLM, or processed by an executor/parser/graph traversal module). Papers' original terms are retained only when introducing specific works. Under this criterion, works producing weakly formalized text artifacts consumed through reading belong to P1; works producing artifacts with structure that is mechanistically exploited (code, workflow, graph) belong to P2 (§3.2); works whose artifacts do not originate from concrete trajectories but are generated by the model from parametric knowledge belong to P7 (§6).

The distinction between P1 and P2 thus hinges on whether structure plays a mechanistic role in downstream reuse—not on surface features like numbered lists, field labels, or section headers that may adorn an otherwise natural-language artifact.

## §3.1 Narrative-to-Narrative Transformation (P1)

P1 transforms Narrative Tokenized agent experience into higher-level Narrative artifacts. The source experience consists of natural-language or multimodal serialized records of how the agent observed, reasoned, acted, and received environmental feedback in specific contexts. P1 does not move experience across carrier levels—both source and target lie within the weakly formalized region of the Tokenized layer. It performs abstraction, compression, rewriting, or induction within that layer. The resulting artifacts take diverse forms—reflections, summaries, lessons, rules, guidelines, insights, causal statements, natural-language skills—unified by a common downstream mechanism: subsequent agents consume them through language or multimodal understanding, entering the decision context via retrieval-augmented prompting or prompt injection, rather than through a parser, executor, graph traversal module, or training process.

P1's internal variation lies not in the surface names of artifacts but in how the experience store is maintained. We first divide P1 methods by store type. **Static Memory Stores** are built once or in batch, after which existing entries are only read; pure append-only accumulation, pre-storage filtering of raw trajectories, per-task temporary caches, and systems that maintain no persistent store at all all fall here. **Dynamic Memory Stores** perform rewriting, merging, pruning, replacement, or versioning on existing entries during continuous agent operation. The distinction is whether existing entries can be retroactively modified—an append-only store with no modification of old entries is Static. Within Static, we further divide by the number of trajectories the abstraction draws upon (single vs. multi). Dynamic stores receive streaming writes (each typically from a single trajectory) and are distinguished by cross-time entry evolution, rendering the single/multi distinction inapplicable.

The P1/P7 boundary is determined by source: P1's input must be concrete agent trajectories or interaction logs; artifacts primarily generated by the model from task descriptions or internalized knowledge without concrete trajectories as input belong to P7.

### §3.1.1 Static Memory Store

Static Memory Store methods treat the products of experience abstraction as read-only assets. The store's concrete form varies: some are per-task temporary caches discarded after the task ends; some are persistent stores built once and never retroactively modified; some filter raw trajectories before abstraction. Their commonality is that entries, once written, are never edited or merged—experience quality is locked in at write time, and the system has no self-correction mechanism over long-term use. These designs are simple to implement and behaviorally predictable, at the cost that erroneous entries, once admitted, persistently affect downstream decisions.

#### §3.1.1.1 Single-Trajectory Experience Abstraction

Single-trajectory abstraction takes one agent trajectory, episode, or local execution log as input and compresses a single concrete execution into a higher-level natural-language artifact. Triggers include task completion, failure occurrence, or trial completion; mechanisms include reflection, summarization, critique, and failure explanation. A single trajectory already carries reusable signals: failure trajectories may reveal incorrect assumptions, ineffective actions, or missing constraints; success trajectories may contain effective decomposition strategies or environment interaction patterns. These methods offer low latency, low data requirements, and independence from historical accumulation, suiting trial-and-error settings, online adaptation, and narrow task distributions. Their limitation is that abstraction relies on a single trajectory, risking the misattribution of contingent factors as generalizable experience, with artifacts strongly bound to the original task context and limited cross-task transferability.

Reflexion [Shi23b] is an early representative of this paradigm. After each trial, the agent generates a brief reflection from the failure trajectory and environmental feedback, writing error attribution and improvement suggestions as verbal memory concatenated into the next round's prompt. Ablation shows that post-hoc reflections are more effective than replaying the raw episodic trajectory. Reflexion's experience store is a classic per-task cache—old entries are not rewritten, only truncated by recency when the context becomes too long. MemOrb [Hua25] compresses each customer-service dialogue and its tool-call trajectory into an Orb, recorded as a sextuple of observation, emotion, outcome, context, and strategic reflection, stored in a retrievable layer for subsequent dialogues; its rewrite component targets queries rather than existing Orb entries. Reflection-Based Memory [Aza25] addresses web navigation by summarizing a single browsing trajectory as a Web-Reflection covering effective steps, site functionality limitations, shortcuts, and failure backtracking points, stored as key-value pairs keyed by task embedding and retrieved as top-k for prompt injection.

REFLECT [Liu23] processes a robot's single-execution multimodal observation stream by first converting RGB-D video, audio, and state records into scene graphs, event-level captions, and subgoal-level summaries, then performing hierarchical summarization and generating failure explanations upon subgoal verification failure to drive correction planning. This work maintains no cross-episode persistent store; the entire pipeline is a one-shot single-trajectory summarization. WebCoach [Liu25e] compresses each completed web episode into a 3--5 sentence summary retaining success status and failure mode fields, stored in an External Memory Store; it persists only completed episodes and discards intermediate processes—this pre-storage filtering does not constitute evolution of stored entries.

Two code-domain works push single-trajectory abstraction toward field-structured experience cards. SWE-Exp [Che25d] extracts comprehension experience and modification experience from a single repair trajectory, forming experience bank entries with perspective, modification, and issue type fields; the paper emphasizes continuous bank expansion but provides no mechanism for editing or merging old entries, placing it in Static under the append-only criterion. MemGovern [Wan26ap] refines Experience Cards from closed-loop repair records of GitHub issues, PRs, and patches through a checklist-style refinement loop, producing Index-layer and Resolution-layer artifacts. MemGovern's experience source is governed human experience rather than agent self-trial—unlike most works in this section, but our inclusion criteria impose no restriction on experience source; human demonstrations are equally legitimate.

Meta-Policy Reflexion [Wu25] writes retrospective reflections on failed episodes as weighted predicate-like rules rather than long-form reflective paragraphs. New rules are incorporated into memory during training rounds, with memory frozen at test time; the agent retrieves rules and performs admissibility checks before injecting them into the prompt. Rules remain unmodified during testing, placing this work in Static; its rule form, more formalized than typical natural-language reflections and approaching a lightweight rule set, still relies on reading rather than execution for reuse, keeping it in P1. Procedural Knowledge at Scale [Wu26] segments teacher-model-synthesized large-scale reasoning traces into subquestion--subroutine pairs, forming a static procedural datastore; at inference time the model verbalizes the current subquestion and retrieves the corresponding subroutine as a hint. Its experience source is teacher-synthesized traces and the experimental form is closer to reasoning than agent--environment interaction, making it peripheral material for this section.

#### §3.1.1.2 Multi-Trajectory Experience Induction

Multi-trajectory induction takes multiple trajectories, demonstrations, or failure cases as input and extracts more stable, more transferable Narrative artifacts through comparison, clustering, or merging. Transferable experience typically arises from stable structures that recur across multiple executions; contrasting success and failure trajectories can identify which behaviors correlate with task completion and which errors recur across contexts. Compared to single-trajectory abstraction, these methods are more likely to filter out the contingent noise of individual trajectories, at the cost of weaker cold-start capability and the risk of over-generalization.

Within the Static category, relatively few works qualify. Most methods performing cross-trajectory induction simultaneously maintain an evolving experience store and thus fall into §3.1.2 under our criterion; works that perform one-shot cross-trajectory induction with subsequent read-only reuse constitute this smaller Static multi-trajectory set.

AutoGuide [Fu24] locates the first decision divergence point from paired success--failure trajectories of the same task, extracts context-bound guidelines around the diverging action, and writes them in when-context-then-action form, organized as a dictionary by context. Each guideline depends on a contrastive pair of one success and one failure trajectory—a classic case of pairwise contrastive extraction. Coarse-to-Fine Grounded Memory [Yan25] first obtains coarse focus points from environmental base information and offline trial-and-error trajectories, then compares success and failure trajectories to extract hybrid-grained tips; its long-term reusable component is primarily the tips dictionary, while fine-grained key information extracted online functions as transient analysis within the current trajectory rather than entering the long-term store.

M2 [Yan26b] places single-trajectory compression and multi-trajectory induction in two memory layers of the same system. Internal memory compresses observation, thought, and action at each step into a brief state summary, recursively concatenated into compact history; an external insight bank uses a strategy abstractor to extract topic-tagged insights from historical success logs. Neither layer modifies existing entries, both qualifying as Static—M2 thus spans single-trajectory (internal memory) and multi-trajectory (insight bank) categories, with internal memory corresponding to single-trajectory compression and the insight bank to cross-log induction.

### §3.1.2 Dynamic Memory Store

Dynamic Memory Store methods treat the experience store as an object that evolves with continued interaction. The system not only writes new experiences but also performs rewriting, merging, pruning, replacement, or versioning on existing entries, endowing the store with self-correction and deduplication capabilities over long-term use. The core design question shifts from "how to generate an artifact from one experience" to how experience enters long-term memory, how existing memories are updated, and how duplicate or obsolete entries are merged or deleted. Dynamic stores receive streaming writes (each typically from a single trajectory); their distinguishing feature is cross-time entry evolution. We organize the discussion by the mechanism of evolution.

One class of work maintains natural-language rule or insight banks and implements editing and voting as explicit memory operations. ExpeL [Zha23c] uses a dedicated insight model to extract transferable insights from success trajectory pools and failure--success pairs, performing ADD, EDIT, UPVOTE, and DOWNVOTE on existing entries; each insight carries an importance score and can be modified or removed as subsequent experience accumulates. H2R [Ye25b] decomposes hindsight reflection into high-level planning memory and low-level execution memory, with both pools supporting add, modify, upvote, and downvote; planner and executor each retrieve from their respective layer. AutoManual [Che24] has a Planner interact with the environment, a Builder differentially extract rules based on three outcome types (direct success, indirect success, failure), a Consolidator merge redundant and delete obsolete rules when they exceed a threshold, and a Formulator compile them into a Markdown manual—a two-stage evolution of continuous writing and consolidation. Agentic Context Engineering [Zha25f] has a Reflector extract concrete insights, failure modes, and strategy bullets from reasoning trajectories, with a Curator merging them as deltas into a Context Playbook; each bullet carries an ID and helpful/harmful count, supported by semantic deduplication and low-utility entry pruning.

Another class drives store convergence through replacement, pruning, or survival scoring. Darwinian Memory [Mi26b] writes each GUI experience as a memory unit indexed by precondition and goal, regulates the memory bank through evolutionary replacement, survival-based pruning, and reputation-style inhibition; upon memory hit during execution, the stored trajectory is directly reused, with epsilon-mutation optionally exploring better versions. R2D2 [Hua25e] truncates, corrects, and annotates web trajectories with reflective insights, forming query-keyed memories; when a new trajectory provides more accurate reflection for the same query, the old value is replaced. Remember Me, Refine Me [Cao25] structures experience entries with usage scenario, confidence, and tools fields, and continuously prunes by utility—its dynamism comes primarily from deletion and filtering rather than verbatim rewriting of entry text. Mistake Notebook Learning [Su25] takes a batch of failure samples as the update unit, first clustering by subject, then generating mistake notes; each update round undergoes full-batch post-update evaluation, and the new notebook version is accepted only if it improves overall performance. Its update is batch rather than online per-entry, and its accept/rollback mechanism is more stringent than common append-only notebooks—it is precisely this controlled version replacement that qualifies it as Dynamic. Hindsight Trajectory Rewriting [Hu25] rewrites a single failure trajectory via hindsight rewriting into a synthetic success workflow targeting an achievable alternative goal, writing it into a replay buffer; when a shorter or better version for the same goal appears, the buffer replaces the old entry.

Skill-oriented works continuously absorb individual experiences into an evolving skill library. Trace2Skill [Ni26] has parallel analysts propose local patches for each success and failure trajectory, then synthesizes transferable skill updates through hierarchical merge and conflict-free consolidation, producing a skill document centered on SKILL.md; existing documents can be further rewritten in deepening mode. AutoSkill [Yan26] extracts skill candidates from user interaction signals, with a prompt-driven judge deciding to add, merge, or discard, and retained skills undergoing versioned evolution; the artifact is SKILL.md with trigger, tags, examples, and version fields. SkillClaw [Ma26b] aggregates real usage sessions from multiple users and agents, with an Agentic Evolver deciding to create, refine, or skip, and valid updates synced back to a shared SkillHub after overnight verification. These three works produce skill files with typed fields, but downstream agents consume them by directly reading the SKILL.md text without a dedicated executor or parser—under the downstream consumption criterion they remain in P1.

Several skill or procedure libraries exhibit higher degrees of structure or multimodality, sitting at the P1/P2 boundary. SkillX [Wan26] builds a three-layer SkillKB (planning, functional, atomic) and continuously optimizes through merge, filter, and exploratory expansion; its planning layer can be written as natural-language steps, while the functional and atomic layers are more strongly structured. Skill Set Optimization [Not24] extracts high-value subtrajectories from actor history, summarizes them as skills with subgoals and numbered instructions, and continuously sifts and replaces the skill set by actual observed value. Memp [Fan25] simultaneously accommodates verbatim trajectory and abstract script forms of procedural memory, executing add, adjust, and delete after success or failure, and can synthesize revised memory from new and old information. GUI-explorer [Xie25] extracts visual keys and corresponding textual operational values for each state transition, merging new values into existing entries when keys match but values differ sufficiently; the artifact is a multimodal entry with visual keys. Mirage-1 [Xie25b] summarizes single GUI trajectories as execution skills, then rolls them up into core skills and meta skills; when new trajectories arrive, a RefineSkillModule performs augmentation and merging. Get Experience from Practice [Fen25] summarizes multi-round interaction traces into high-level procedural experience, low-level execution experience, and accompanying check functions, performing updating, merging, and ranking in a repository; its low-level items can be directly converted to API calls or scripts—this component clearly carries executability, diverging from pure narrative artifacts and approaching P2.

Another class of work maintains multiple semantically distinct evolving stores in parallel. Learning on the Job [Yan25d] converts long-horizon interactions into strategic memory (dilemma--strategy pairs), procedural memory (SOPs), and tool memory (operation tips), performing unified refinement, integration, and deduplication after each task. What Deserves Memory [Ma25] uses the prediction error between raw episodes and anticipatory schemas to determine which information merits retention, distilling it into semantic knowledge and fusing consecutive episodes into narrative episodic memory, with both layers executing merge and conflict replacement. CLIN [Maj23] extracts causal linguistic statements from each trial (e.g., that an action is necessary for an outcome or does not contribute) and generates cross-episode meta-memory from high-quality memories, producing an updated version after every trial. Contextual Experience Replay [Liu25] distills each experience into environment dynamics and skills summaries, explicitly referencing existing buffer content during writing to avoid duplication—current writes are thus constrained by historical integration state.

Dynamic Cheatsheet [Suz25] updates an entire document rather than individual entries. A Curator evaluates the usefulness and generalizability of problem-solving trajectories accumulated during testing, distills transferable content into a cheatsheet, and performs rewriting, deletion, merging, and compression on its entries to control length—its update granularity is coarser than the typical entry-wise memory store.

### §3.1.3 Discussion

Static and Dynamic stores correspond to a clear set of trade-offs. Static stores are simple to implement, behaviorally predictable, and incur low runtime overhead, but experience quality is locked at write time, the system lacks self-correction, and a single miswritten reflection will persistently affect subsequent retrieval. Dynamic stores converge through merge, replacement, and pruning over long-term use, better filtering the contingent noise of single abstractions, at the cost of introducing additional mechanisms (conflict resolution, staleness detection, versioning), making behavior harder to reproduce, and risking that pruning strategies themselves may delete still-useful entries.

Within Static, the single-trajectory vs. multi-trajectory contrast is likewise a trade-off. Single-trajectory abstraction has low latency, low data requirements, and good cold-start performance, suiting narrow task distributions and rapid adaptation, but the contingency and misattribution of a single trajectory directly enter the experience store. Multi-trajectory induction achieves stronger generalization and noise resistance through cross-trajectory comparison, at the cost of weaker cold-start and the need for applicability-condition modeling when different trajectories support conflicting conclusions. The small number of Static multi-trajectory works is itself a noteworthy observation: cross-trajectory induction, once initiated, tends to be accompanied by continuous store evolution; purely static cross-trajectory induction constitutes a narrow design space.

Experience sources in P1 are predominantly agent self-generated trajectories; MemGovern's human repair records and Wu26's teacher-synthesized traces are exceptions. Experience source is orthogonal to the Static/Dynamic division and does not alter pathway attribution. Multimodality enters P1 through orthogonal attribute tags: REFLECT processes robot multimodal observation streams; GUI-explorer and Mirage-1 carry visual keys in skill entries. These works do not follow separate pathways due to modality differences; they exhibit implementation differences within their respective categories.

Taken together, P1 methods form a spectrum from local to global, from static to dynamic: Single-Trajectory Abstraction provides the lowest-latency, lowest-data-requirement local adaptation; Multi-Trajectory Induction provides stronger generalization and stability; Dynamic Memory Consolidation represents the more systematic direction within P1, connecting single-experience abstraction, multi-experience integration, and long-term memory management, enabling agents to progressively build a sustainably reusable Narrative experience store from isolated execution logs.

## §3.2 Narrative-to-Schematic Transformation (P2)

P2 transforms Narrative Tokenized agent experience into Schematic Tokenized artifacts with explicit structural constraints. The source experience consists of concrete trajectories from agent sequential decision-making—task execution logs, reasoning traces, tool-use trajectories, web navigation histories, GUI interaction traces, or embodied execution records. The core operation is formalizing weakly formalized Narrative experience into structured artifacts that can be parsed, executed, traversed, instantiated, or scheduled. Typical target carriers include executable code skills, parameterized action programs, workflows, DAGs, hierarchical procedures, task trees, knowledge graphs, state-transition graphs, and structured memory graphs.

A work qualifies as P2 only if it satisfies three conditions simultaneously: the input is a concrete agent trajectory or interaction log (distinguishing it from P7, where the model generates artifacts from parametric knowledge); the output is a Schematic artifact with parseable, executable, or traversable structure; and downstream reuse mechanistically exploits this structure rather than merely having an LLM freely read its textual surface. The third condition is the critical boundary with P1—whether structure plays a mechanistic role in downstream decision-making.

We classify P2 methods by the primary semantic function the resulting Schematic artifact serves in subsequent agent decision-making: **Programmatic Skill Construction** (experience transformed into callable action units), **Procedural Workflow Induction** (experience transformed into multi-step task process structures), and **Structured Memory Graph Construction** (experience transformed into graph-structured representations of environments, states, or interaction relationships). For graph-shaped artifacts, classification follows semantic function rather than surface topology: if the graph primarily encodes task execution order, step dependencies, control flow, or phase structure, it belongs to Procedural Workflow Induction; if it primarily encodes entities, states, spatial relationships, environmental knowledge, or interaction history, it belongs to Structured Memory Graph Construction. Works whose artifacts span multiple semantic functions, or whose P2 attribution is itself ambiguous, are discussed centrally in §3.2.4.

### §3.2.1 Programmatic Skill Construction

Programmatic Skill Construction builds callable, executable, or parameterizable skill units from concrete agent trajectories, enabling the agent to directly invoke these skills in subsequent tasks rather than re-planning from low-level actions. Source experience is typically successful or filtered execution trajectories; transformed artifacts take the form of code skills, parameterized action programs, or callable skill interfaces. These artifacts serve "action compression" or "action space expansion": behavior that originally required multiple low-level operations is abstracted into a single high-level callable unit, typically executable by a runtime, browser automation framework, or environment interface.

The boundary between §3.2.1 and §3.2.2 does not depend on whether the artifact internally contains multi-step processes or pre/post-conditions—both features are common in both categories. The criterion is the artifact's scheduling status in downstream decision-making: a Programmatic Skill is a whole, selectable, startable, executable action unit; the agent selects and executes it as a single high-level action in the action space. A Procedural Workflow is a process structure that organizes the execution of an entire class of tasks; the planner navigates among its phases, determining the current phase from observations. A skill can serve as a node within a workflow.

**Representative works.** Voyager [Wan23c] iteratively generates, executes, and repairs action programs in Minecraft through iterative prompting; only programs that pass environmental feedback, execution errors, and self-verification are written into the skill library. The library is indexed by skill description embeddings, with values being executable Mineflayer JavaScript programs; new tasks retrieve top-k skills by task plan for compositional invocation. ASI [Wan25d] starts from episodes judged successful by an evaluator during online problem solving, cleanses error steps, induces typed Python functions via an induction module, rewrites the original trajectory as a version calling the new skill, and re-executes—only skills whose rewritten trajectories reproduce success are admitted to the library, binding trajectory abstraction to execution-grounded re-verification. SkillWeaver [Zhe25c] lets a web agent practice autonomously under an automated curriculum, identifies successful behaviors with a reward model, prompts an LLM to generate typed Playwright APIs from state-action sequences, and continuously hones them through unit tests, static analysis, and environmental feedback; generated skills carry function signatures, docstrings, and prerequisite states, joining the action space as high-level APIs. WebXSkill [Wan26d] takes synthetic traces from a stronger teacher model executed on WebArena and WebVoyager as input, extracts action subsequences, abstracts them into JSON skills with typed parameters and step-level guidance, and organizes them by URL pattern into a skill graph; skills can be invoked atomically (grounded mode) or used as step-by-step instructions injected into the prompt (guided mode)—despite internal step sequences, the whole is retrieved and used as a single callable skill unit.

Skill-Pro [Mi26] produces artifacts in natural language rather than code yet still belongs here: it extracts semantic gradients from success and failure trajectories through hindsight attribution, proposes new skills after verifying counterfactual advantage via a PPO-style trust-region gate, with the artifact being an (activation condition, execution procedure, termination condition) triple. The downstream agent first selects a skill in a Skill-MDP, then generates primitive actions under that skill's constraints with a frozen LLM. It is a whole, selectable, startable action unit—this scheduling status, not the code form, determines its classification as skill.

PSN [Shi26] and AutoRefine [Qiu26] produce artifacts that span both skill and workflow functions. PSN represents skills as symbolic programs with executable control flow, preconditions, postconditions, and CHILDREN call relationships; the agent performs backward-chaining planning on a skill network. It is not the purest case of one-shot artifact distillation from trajectories, but rather uses continuous execution traces to drive failure credit assignment, local patching, redundancy merging, and online structural refactoring of the skill network—a "trace-driven evolution" type. AutoRefine performs contrastive extraction across success and failure trajectories, producing Skill Patterns (natural-language guidelines or code snippets with steps, closer to skills) and Subagent Patterns (specialized subagents with independent system prompts, reasoning loops, and internal memory, used via hierarchical delegation, functionally closer to subtask scheduling within workflows). The cross-functional nature of both works is noted in §3.2.4.

Programmatic Skill Construction's advantages are executability, verifiability, and composability: artifacts can be validated by execution results, and multiple skills can be composed for complex tasks. Its costs are high sensitivity to environment interface stability, difficulty in determining skill generalization boundaries, and the need for selection, deduplication, versioning, and stale detection mechanisms as the skill library grows—PSN's maturity-aware update gating and SkillWeaver's API selection module respond precisely to these challenges.

### §3.2.2 Procedural Workflow Induction

Procedural Workflow Induction abstracts reusable task process structures from multi-step agent trajectories: workflows, DAGs, hierarchical procedures, task trees, or stage-action hierarchies. These methods abstract not single callable actions but the phase decomposition, step ordering, dependency relationships, control flow, conditional branching, and pre/post-conditions needed to complete a class of tasks. Much reusable experience in complex tasks is embodied in how task execution is organized: success trajectories often contain stable phase patterns (gather information, filter candidates, verify constraints, submit); failure trajectories may reveal missing step ordering or dependency checks. Compared to Programmatic Skills, workflows operate at a higher granularity—a single skill can be a node in a workflow, and a workflow can schedule multiple skills, tools, or subtasks.

**Representative works.** Agent Workflow Memory [Wan24] extracts recurring sub-routines from a web agent's canonical experience, replacing instance values with placeholders; the artifact is a workflow trajectory composed of environment description, reasoning, and action program. It supports both offline (human-annotated or model-synthesized experience) and online (induction from evaluator-judged positive trajectories) modes; workflows can be appended to the memory prompt for reference or wrapped as high-level functions joining the action space. Its carrier form is natural language text, but the step skeleton is explicit and downstream reuse does follow that structure—a workflow case within P2 whose carrier is textual. WorkflowGen [Wei26] extracts experience at both node and workflow granularities, clustering structurally identical trajectories into generalized workflow templates with nodes marked fixed or generatable; at inference, similarity determines a three-level routing—high similarity reuses directly, medium rewrites only variable nodes, low similarity re-plans. Hierarchical Memory Tree [Tan26] segments successful web trajectories into subgoal segments, constructing a three-layer tree of intent / stage / action; the stage layer carries explicit pre/post-conditions, leaf layers replace DOM identifiers with transferable semantic element descriptions; the Planner aligns the current observation with stage conditions to determine the current phase, and the Actor grounds abstract actions to the page—specifically addressing workflow mismatch. For25 [For25] segments trajectories into atomic procedures (goal, precondition, action sequence, postcondition quadruples), further abstracts repeatedly co-successful procedure sequences into meta-procedures with continue/skip/repeat/abort control logic; at runtime, Bayesian expected utility ranks and selects procedures, with contrastive refinement and reliability-based pruning after success/failure accumulation. Meta-Agent-Workflow [Tan25b] maps optimal tool-utilization trajectories to tool invocation templates, parameterizes instances into JSON DSL workflows targeting the Dify platform, and upon execution errors locates the faulty node for LLM-generated revision plans that reconstruct the corresponding DSL block.

MOBIMEM [Liu25i] organizes experience across both workflow and graph structures; we assign it here primarily for its workflow component. It distills mobile execution experience and user corrections into multi-level experience templates with parameter slots (high-level templates encode task-level control flow, low-level preserve concrete UI steps), with a Task Rewriter matching and filling slots to produce executable plans. Its graph components (DisGraph organizing user profile conceptual relationships, ActTree caching prefix paths) are noted in §3.2.4. MOBIMEM is a self-evolution memory system coupling template synthesis, exception-driven updating, and rollback-based stale detection; its contribution lies in component integration rather than any single transformation, making it a candidate for Composite (§7). We assign a single primary attribution to avoid repeated treatment across §3.2's three subsections.

A2Flow [Zha25c] takes expert demonstrations and task-resolution data, converts case-level demonstrations into code-based workflows and initial operators, performs operator clustering to obtain task-aware abstract operators, and uses MCTS to optimize the overall process in a search space of nodes, edges, and operators; the artifact is a directly executable directed workflow graph. Its source is human demonstration (a permitted {human} source), its output is an explicit workflow, but MCTS optimization tilts it toward workflow synthesis more than pure experience induction, making it a boundary case within this category.

FlowMind [Liu26] employs a two-phase Execute--Summarize approach: first, the model completes tasks using tools and records execution traces; then, in a separate summarize phase, a graph-building tool extracts essential steps, dependencies, and control flow, forming a workflow graph with sequencing, branching, and iteration. Its transformation (trace → structured workflow graph) satisfies all three P2 conditions, but the artifact primarily serves reproduction, debugging, auditing, and downstream automated system invocation rather than as a continuously retrieved and reused agent memory; if this category's scope is limited to "sustainably reusable workflow memory," FlowMind is a boundary case.

Procedural Workflow Induction's advantages are interpretability and controllability: workflow artifacts are shorter and more abstract, explicitly expressing step dependencies and execution order, facilitating the localization of critical task phases. The main challenges are abstraction granularity control—too concrete and generalization suffers, too abstract and critical context is lost—and workflow drift: when environmental state changes, the original workflow may become invalid, requiring consistency checking, precondition verification, and update mechanisms. Hierarchical Memory Tree's fallback on low confidence and WorkflowGen's three-level routing are designs targeting precisely this problem.

### §3.2.3 Structured Memory Graph Construction

Structured Memory Graph Construction transforms entities, relationships, state changes, spatial structures, event dependencies, or interaction histories from agent trajectories into retrievable, traversable, updatable graph-structured memory. These methods do not aim to produce executable actions or task workflows, but rather to construct structured representations of the agent's environment, task world, historical experience, or multi-agent interaction relationships. Much agent experience is poorly suited to expression as linear text or fixed procedures, and better organized as relational structures: environmental objects have spatial relationships, actions change states, historical events have temporal or causal connections, and multi-agent interactions involve roles, tasks, information flows, and dependencies. Storing these relationships only as raw logs or natural-language memory notes makes precise retrieval, local updating, and multi-hop reasoning difficult for subsequent agents.

**Representative works.** AriGraph [Ano24] extracts semantic triplets from each textual observation step in a text-game, adding them to a semantic memory, while simultaneously recording the full observation as an episodic vertex linked to that step's triplets, forming a knowledge graph world model encoding both world knowledge and episodic history; downstream, semantic search is performed first, then episodic links are followed to retrieve relevant past observations for working memory, with old relationships deleted when new observations overturn them. G-Memory [Zha25] targets multi-agent systems, organizing atomic utterances from collaboration into an interaction graph, instantiating full queries as query nodes, and having an LLM summarize insight nodes, forming three layers (interaction, query, insight). On a new query, bidirectional traversal is performed—upward for high-level insights, downward for sparsified successful collaboration fragments—with role-specific pruning before injection into each agent's context. A-MEM [Xu25b] converts each interaction into an atomic note with keyword, tag, contextual description, and embedding, establishing semantic relationships between new and old notes through link generation; new experiences also trigger rewriting of old notes. Retrieval first recalls top-k notes by vector similarity, then expands along linked memory and boxes to retrieve related notes. It encodes relation structure rather than process structure, placing it in the graph branch; its links serve downstream both as retrieval expansion paths and as a relational organization dimension, differing from typical environment graphs (§3.2.4). MobileGPT [Lee23] converts completed mobile app tasks into a task / sub-task / primitive action hierarchy through an Explore--Select--Derive process, organizing pages and subtask relationships as a transition graph (nodes are functionally equivalent app pages, edges are reusable sub-tasks carrying action sequences); new instructions are first recalled to align with a memory task path, with matched results executed directly following the graph structure. Its core artifact is a transition graph of page nodes and sub-task edges, placing it in graph; edges internally carry process steps, intersecting with the workflow subcategory.

Environment Maps [Fen26] and BrainMem [Ma26] produce artifacts that span graph and other functions. Environment Maps compiles browser traces, DOM, and screenshots into a JSON environment map containing context nodes, parameterized action nodes, workflow order, and tacit knowledge; the agent queries through Read/Grep/Glob file interfaces. Its workflow field and sequential edge do encode process order, but the core is a structured representation of the environment and actionable elements, so graph is the primary attribution. BrainMem maintains dual graphs in an embodied setting—a Trajectory KG (action-state transitions) and a Spatial KG (room-object spatial relationships)—with an Experience Agent refining successful trajectories into generalizable patterns after each episode and compressing failure trajectories into symbolic guidelines through guided retry. The dual KGs are P2 graph artifacts, while the episode-level semantic guidelines are P1 Narrative artifacts—BrainMem thus spans P1 and P2; we attribute it here for its graph component, with its guideline distillation cross-referenced in §3.1.

The distinction between Structured Memory Graph and Procedural Workflow lies in the graph's semantic function: a workflow graph answers "how should the task be executed," with nodes and edges representing step order, control flow, or task dependencies; a memory graph answers "what exists in the environment, how states change, what relationships exist among historical experiences," with nodes and edges representing entities, states, events, spatial, or interaction relationships. The advantage of this class is relation-level memory reuse; graph structures facilitate local updating and suit the long-horizon planning and state tracking needs of embodied agents, web agents, and multi-agent systems. The difficulties are that graph extraction readily produces erroneous entities and relationships, and that graph memory over time suffers from memory bloat, stale information, and retrieval noise, typically requiring accompanying mechanisms for graph update, conflict resolution, pruning, and confidence estimation—AriGraph's conflicting-relationship deletion and G-Memory's graph sparsification are representative mechanisms.

### §3.2.4 Boundary Cases and Scope Clarification

Several works annotated within §3.2.1–§3.2.3 span multiple semantic functions: PSN and AutoRefine span skill and workflow (primary attribution §3.2.1), MOBIMEM spans workflow and graph (primary attribution §3.2.2), Environment Maps and BrainMem span graph and other functions (primary attribution §3.2.3). All receive a single primary attribution with cross-functionality noted in their primary subsection and in this centralized discussion; §8 cross-pathway comparisons count them by their primary attribution. BrainMem's semantic guideline distillation belongs to P1, making it a work that genuinely spans P1/P2.

**Hybrid schematic-narrative artifacts.** Agent KB [Tan25] constructs a cross-domain experience base from heterogeneous trajectories across multiple agent frameworks, extracting unified experience units $E=\langle\pi,\gamma,S,C\rangle$ through human-guided abstraction, where $\gamma$ is a structured predicate and $S$ is an action-reasoning pair; the application phase synthesizes retrieved experiences into a preliminary execution plan and targeted fix through Reason--Retrieve--Refine. Its experience unit simultaneously contains schematic components (structured predicates, numbered agent_planning) and narrative components (principled agent_experience text), and is not a clean SOP, DSL, or DAG. With self-evolving memory (new entries added, LLM ranker deduplication, recency/frequency/transfer-success-based eviction), its structural form qualifies as weak P2; if §3.2.2's scope is restricted to explicitly strongly formalized workflow artifacts, Agent KB is more safely treated as a hybrid boundary case.

This case illustrates a general principle: surface fields and numbering do not equal Schematic. Some works summarize trajectories as numbered workflows, SOPs, or guidelines, yet downstream consumption still primarily relies on LLM natural-language understanding with no parser or graph traversal involved—such works belong to P1. AutoSkill, SkillClaw, and SkillX, retained in §3.1 as "programmatic-leaning skill consolidation," exemplify this: their carriers approach typed skill libraries, but downstream consumption relies on LLM reading comprehension, keeping them out of P2.

**Non-agent and human-software-trace sources.** A body of work constructs workflows from low-level event streams of human software use; their boundary with the P2 main taxonomy depends on whether the source data possesses the experience loop of LLM-based agent sequential decision-making. From Logs to Agents [Jo26] reconstructs creative workflow DAGs from raw system traces (CSV/JSON event streams) of creative software for Markov-style process analysis and process-aware agent context. In-Context Ensemble Learning [Xu24b] aggregates step-ordered SOPs from WONDERBREAD human demonstration videos for workflow understanding and documentation. Workflow Graphs [Cha20] constructs W-graphs from instrumented Tinkercad human 3D modeling traces and predates LLMs. These three have source data from human software usage records rather than LLM agent interaction trajectories, with goals oriented toward process understanding, documentation, or population-level strategy analysis; they are not included in the P2 main taxonomy. Cha20's W-graph can be cited as a methodological precursor for "human traces → workflow graph"; Jo26 and Xu24b serve as external references for workflow reconstruction. These should be distinguished from the {human} sources our inclusion criteria permit: Agent Workflow Memory's offline human-annotated experience and A2Flow's expert demonstrations remain in P2 because the demonstrations themselves carry decision-process semantics and heterogeneous action spaces. The exclusion criterion is whether the source data carry sequential decision-making semantics, independent of whether the source is human or agent.

**Source-side ambiguity.** Some works generate code, workflows, or graphs, but the artifacts are not extracted from concrete trajectories—rather, the model generates them from task descriptions, expert knowledge, or parametric capabilities. These are closer to P7 or general program/workflow synthesis and are not counted in P2.

**Composite pipelines.** Some systems first convert trajectories into Schematic artifacts, then use them for data synthesis, SFT, or RL. If the paper's core contribution is the internalization of experience into parameters with the Schematic artifact serving only as an intermediate representation, such works belong to Composite (§7) or the Tokenized-to-Parametric pathways. The P2 chapter may reference them to illustrate the mediating role of Schematic artifacts but does not treat them as pure P2 methods.
