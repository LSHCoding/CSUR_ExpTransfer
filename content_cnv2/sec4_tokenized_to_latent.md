# §4 Tokenized-to-Latent Transformations (P3)

P3 transforms Tokenized agent experience into continuous representations directly consumable by the model—KV cache, hidden states, soft prompts, prefix vectors, memory token embeddings, latent memory banks, or cross-attention memory outputs. The target carrier is no longer discrete tokens but internal computational states that directly participate in attention, hidden-state computation, or action prediction. Experience, when reused, does not return to natural language or other discrete token forms but is re-invoked as model-native state.

P3 is fundamentally distinct from RAG-style memory. In RAG, embeddings serve as a retrieval index, but what the model ultimately consumes is the retrieved text—the embedding is merely the retrieval mechanism; the carrier in which experience is actually reused remains Tokenized text. P3 requires the transformed experience to enter attention or hidden-state computation directly as continuous representation, without being decoded back to natural language or other discrete tokens before entering the model.

P3 also differs from mere inference efficiency optimization. Many methods for KV cache eviction, attention sparsification, cache quantization, or serving-level cache reuse likewise operate on KV caches or hidden states, but their goal is primarily to reduce memory footprint, latency, or throughput cost, and they do not necessarily carry cross-turn, cross-session, cross-task, or long-horizon episode experience reuse semantics. We only include methods that explicitly convert historical interaction into reusable Latent artifacts—the Latent representation must serve the function of experience storage and reuse, rather than being merely a transient acceleration structure within an inference system.

The central differentiating factor among P3 methods is where the reusable Latent representation comes from. Along the axis of "dedicated mechanism invested to obtain the representation," P3 methods form a progressive spectrum, divided into three classes. **Cache-Based Latent Transformation** performs no additional training, directly reusing real KV/hidden states produced by historical forward passes. **Prompt-Based Latent Transformation** compresses experience through training into static continuous prompt representations (soft prompts, prefix vectors, memory token embeddings), with the base model kept frozen. **Module-Based Latent Transformation** constructs, through training, a dynamic Latent memory system with explicit write, read, and fusion operations. The three classes progress along "reuse existing computation → train static latent → train dynamic latent system," with dedicated mechanism and expressive power increasing and training/engineering cost rising in the same direction.

## §4.1 Cache-Based Latent Transformation

Cache-Based Latent Transformation preserves KV caches, hidden states, or other attention-level computations produced during historical interactions, directly reusing them in subsequent tasks or time steps. The core idea is that an agent's historical experience exists not only in readable trajectory logs but has already been encoded by the model into internal continuous states while processing those Tokenized experiences. The system can directly reuse the Latent computation formed during past forward passes, rather than re-reading text logs and recomputing them in future tasks.

**Representative works.** Log-Augmented Generation [Che25b] preserves historical reasoning logs as reusable KV caches. At inference, the system first retrieves relevant logs by semantic similarity, then performs position alignment on the KV states within them and injects them into the current `past_key_values`, thereby letting the model inherit the internal computation corresponding to past reasoning chains—rather than re-reading the log text and re-executing the same forward propagation. What is reused is no longer the readable surface form of the reasoning trace but the attention-level computation already formed within the Transformer.

TempoFit [Sun26] advances this approach to the embodied setting. In long-horizon VLA manipulation, the method continuously maintains layer-wise temporal KV memory, preserving prefix KV states corresponding to past visual observations and language conditions in a temporal cache. It retrieves relevant historical states through key matching with temporal bias and writes them back to a frozen VLA as a pre-attention residual. Unlike typical serving-oriented KV reuse, the cache here serves the function of cross-time state inheritance and experience continuity.

Cache-Based methods' advantage is low training cost and high information fidelity—they can directly inherit the fine-grained internal computation the model has already completed. Their limitations are strong coupling to specific backbones, position encodings, and attention implementations; weak interpretability and editability of latent states; and large capacity overhead, requiring accompanying mechanisms for selection, retrieval, compression, eviction, and position alignment.

## §4.2 Prompt-Based Latent Transformation

Prompt-Based Latent Transformation converts demonstrations, reasoning traces, task procedures, or other Tokenized experience into continuous prompt representations—soft prompts, prefix vectors, or memory token embeddings—through training. The base model is typically kept frozen; only these external continuous vectors are trained. Unlike §4.1, which directly reuses KV produced by real forward passes, the continuous representations here are obtained through optimization of a training objective—even when their form likewise manifests as layer-wise KV (as in ReasonCACHE below), their origin is an optimization process rather than a specific inference run. These methods can be seen as a lightweight experience transformation modality between textual prompting and full fine-tuning: more compact than text prompts, not directly bounded by natural-language context length; yet more modular than parameter fine-tuning, facilitating loading different Latent prompts by task, skill, or scenario.

**Representative works.** MAP-VLA [Li25g] segments robotic manipulation demonstrations by phase, learning a set of memory prompts for each stage. The base VLA remains frozen; at inference, the system matches the current execution segment to the most similar historical phase and adds the corresponding soft prompt to the base prompt embedding, allowing demonstration experience to participate in action generation as continuous prompts rather than text exemplars. Experience is not written into policy weights but resides in external, loadable Latent prompts.

ReasonCACHE [Gup26] compresses large-scale reasoning demonstrations into trainable prefix KV pairs at each layer. At test time, the model no longer needs to read lengthy exemplars but instead invokes this experience directly in the attention prefix. Compared to few-shot prompting, experience reuse no longer depends on long text contexts; compared to fine-tuning, experience is not fully absorbed into the main model parameters, remaining as a relatively independent continuous prefix.

TokMem [Wu25c] compresses procedural knowledge into a single memory token embedding. Each token simultaneously functions as a routable discrete index and a continuous control vector: at inference, the model first selects the most relevant memory token by query, then uses that token to activate the corresponding procedure. The procedure is no longer read as a natural-language rule or textual SOP, but participates in generation as an embedding-level control unit.

Prompt-Based methods' shared advantages are high compression ratios and deployment flexibility, suiting experience patterns with clear boundaries and high repetitiveness. Their limitations are that the internal semantics of latent prompts are difficult to interpret, generalization capability depends on training data coverage, and strong coupling to a specific model's embedding space makes cross-model transfer non-trivial.

## §4.3 Module-Based Latent Transformation

Module-Based Latent Transformation dynamically converts, organizes, reads, and updates historical experience into Latent memory through explicit memory modules, memory banks, compressors, composers, cross-attention readers, or structured memory graphs. Unlike the preceding two classes, these methods construct a more complete Latent memory system, enabling the agent to dynamically access and integrate experience according to the current context, historical trajectory, visual state, or task progress.

Experience reuse typically involves explicit write, read, and fusion processes. In the write phase, the system encodes historical trajectories, multi-turn interactions, visual observations, or action feedback into latent entries, stored in a memory bank or episodic buffer. In the read phase, the current query, task state, or visual observation serves as the retrieval signal, selecting relevant experience from memory. In the fusion phase, the retrieved Latent memory influences subsequent generation or action prediction through cross-attention, gating, residual connection, or action-conditioning mechanisms.

Under this common skeleton, Module-Based methods can be further distinguished along two dimensions. The first is when Latent memory is produced: one group persists experience as latent entries during an offline phase, directly retrieving them at read time (e.g., MemoryVLA, Chameleon's latent episodic bank); the other preserves history externally in Tokenized form and compresses it into latent only upon retrieval at inference time—the transformation occurs at query time rather than write time (e.g., LatentMem first retrieves historical trajectories, then compresses them through a composer). The second dimension is memory channel structure: some works maintain a single latent memory, while others explicitly split experience into "perceptual" and "cognitive/temporal" dual channels maintained and read separately—Dual Latent Memory's perception/thinking, MemoryVLA's perceptual/cognitive, and EchoVLA's scene/episodic all exemplify this dual-channel pattern.

**Representative works.** LatentMem [Fu26] retrieves historical multi-agent trajectories from an experience bank, then uses a role-aware memory composer to compress these Tokenized trajectories into an agent-specific latent memory matrix, directly concatenated into the current agent's hidden states for reasoning. The same segment of historical experience can be re-represented and injected according to different agent roles. Dual Latent Memory for Visual Multi-agent System [Yu26d] splits multi-agent experience into latent perception memory and latent thinking memory channels, injected into hidden states when the current agent's decoding is uncertain, transmitting past agents' observations and thinking as latent communication.

In embodied and VLA settings, MemoryVLA [Shi25] constructs a dual-channel Perceptual-Cognitive Memory Bank, fusing historical perceptual and cognitive latents into current action prediction through cross-attention and gating. HAMLET [Koo25] uses moment tokens to extract compact latent summaries at each time step, then aggregates historical token caches through a lightweight Transformer, providing an explicit history-aware state for an originally memoryless VLA. Gated Memory Policy [Gao26] encodes historical images and action trajectories into cached states and uses an explicit memory gate to decide when these latent history tokens should enter the policy through cross-attention, making whether past experience participates in current control a learned, regulated process.

When tasks extend to longer horizons and stronger spatial dependencies, Chameleon [Guo26] writes multi-view visual and proprioceptive history into hierarchical spatiotemporal slot memory, reading out a decision state from the latent episodic bank through state-space dynamics. EchoVLA [Lin25d] maintains dual-layer declarative memory—scene memory and episodic memory—fusing spatial structure and temporal experience as conditions for a diffusion policy through coarse-to-fine cross-attention. Compared to textual memory, these methods can preserve more fine-grained information at the visual, spatial, and action levels, particularly suiting highly non-Markovian tasks like long-horizon robotic manipulation and mobile manipulation.

Not all related work falls purely on the Latent pathway. Hybrid Self-evolving Structured Memory for GUI Agents [Zhu26] simultaneously maintains high-level strategy and attribute tags as Schematic Tokenized memory, and low-level trajectory embeddings as Latent memory, indicating that in GUI agents experience reuse can proceed in parallel along both Tokenized (Schematic) and Latent carrier types. Since these two memory types are maintained independently without an integration mechanism coupling them, we do not treat this as a Composite Pipeline, noting it only as a boundary illustration of Latent and Tokenized coexistence.

Module-Based Latent Transformation is the most expressive method form within P3, particularly suiting long-horizon, multimodal, embodied, GUI, and multi-agent scenarios. Its advantages are strong expressive power and good dynamic adaptability, tightly coupling memory access with current decision-making. Its limitations are the highest system complexity—requiring additional memory modules, cross-attention layers, compressors, or gating mechanisms, with high training and deployment costs; continuous updating of Latent memory introduces staleness, capacity saturation, latent drift, and erroneous experience accumulation.

## §4.4 Discussion

P3 marks a shift in agent memory from "write the past down and read it back" to "encode the past into internal states that can directly participate in computation." In this process, the readability, editability, and interpretability of experience progressively decline, while inference efficiency, intra-model coupling, and fine-grained information retention correspondingly rise. The three classes form a continuous spectrum: Cache-Based methods are closest to direct reuse of raw internal computation, with the lowest training cost but also the strongest dependency on specific backbones; Prompt-Based methods provide a lighter-weight, modular form of Latent adaptation, suitable for compressing stable skills or reasoning priors; Module-Based methods approach system-level Latent memory mechanisms, capable of supporting long-term, multimodal, dynamically updated experience organization, but facing higher modeling and engineering complexity.

The three classes also exhibit a clear gradient in verifiability and robustness. Once written, Latent memory cannot easily be directly inspected or point-fixed by humans; whether the experience is correct or stale can typically only be indirectly inferred from downstream behavior. This non-inspectability increases in degree across the three classes: Cache-Based reuses real KV from a single inference, whose content is still traceable to a specific historical segment; Prompt-Based's static vectors, compressed through training, can no longer be mapped to individual experiences; Module-Based's dynamic memory, continuously written and updated, is the hardest to audit and uniquely suffers from failure modes including staleness, capacity saturation, latent drift, and erroneous experience accumulation—erroneous experience entering latent memory is hard to discover and cannot be directly deleted or edited like a text rule. P3's main robustness cost is concentrated at the Module-Based end.

Within the overall framework, P3 occupies the position between externalized reuse (through Tokenized carriers) and fully parametric reuse (through Parametric carriers). P3's key transition concerns the *form* in which experience is reused, rather than its storage location: Cache-Based external KV memory and Module-Based latent memory banks remain structurally isomorphic to RAG in storage-retrieval terms—experience still resides outside the model body. The difference is that retrieved experience enters attention or hidden-state computation directly as continuous representation, without being decoded back to discrete tokens through tokenization. It is precisely this attention-consumable consumption form that makes P3 a transitional morphology toward Parametric carriers, and explains why it has become increasingly important in embodied, GUI, and multi-agent settings that heavily depend on perceptual grounding and dynamic experience integration.
