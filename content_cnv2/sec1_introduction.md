# §1 Introduction

The transition from static large models (LLMs, VLMs) to autonomous agents introduces a fundamental shift: agents operate within a continuous *experience loop*—they attempt tasks under specific decision contexts, produce heterogeneous actions (reasoning traces, tool invocations, environment controls), observe objective consequences returned by the environment, and optionally receive evaluative feedback. These accumulated decision-level records constitute *agent experience*, initially existing as raw, possibly multimodal, logs.

Retaining raw logs and reusing them through retrieval (e.g., RAG) is intuitive but scales poorly under recent trends such as long-horizon agents, test-time search, and process-level supervision: context window limits impede long-term retention, retrieval latency slows decision-making, and per-episode noise hinders cross-task generalization. In response, the community has developed diverse mechanisms for storing and reusing experience, each corresponding to a different carrier form and exhibiting distinct trade-offs across reusability, verifiability, inference cost, and robustness. No universally optimal carrier exists; the right choice depends on downstream task constraints.

This survey departs from conventional taxonomies organized by "component" (Memory, Planning, Tool Use) or "technique" (SFT, RAG, RLHF). Instead, we take **Experience Transformation and Utilization** as the central axis. Memory, evaluator, and training are reframed as representation-to-representation pathways through which the same semantic record migrates across different carriers. Under this unified framework, we compare their trade-offs and analyze how LLM-based agents transform interaction experience into different carrier forms and reuse them across diverse decision-making scenarios.

Concretely, we define the minimal semantic unit of experience as a modality-agnostic quadruple $e = (c, a, o, f)$, corresponding to Context, Action, Observation, and Feedback. Experience carriers are classified into three top-level categories by their locus in the model architecture: **Tokenized** (discrete token sequences occupying the context window), **Latent** (continuous vectors or hidden states participating in attention computation), and **Parametric** (fully implicit, solidified in network weights). Within Tokenized, we further distinguish Narrative (weakly formalized, consumed via language/multimodal understanding) from Schematic (strongly formalized, consumed via parsing, execution, or graph traversal). Within Parametric, we distinguish Policy (actor weights that generate actions) from Evaluator (judge weights that assess actions).

Experience Transformation is defined as the migration process $\mathcal{T}: \mathcal{C}_{\text{src}} \rightarrow \mathcal{C}_{\text{tgt}}$ between carriers, subject to two conditions: the source content must be traceable to agent experience records (Experience Grounding), and the target carrier must encode the semantic content of the source experience (Experience Embodiment).

Under this framework, we identify 7 foundational transformation pathways, grouped by source carrier into four clusters:

- **Intra-Tokenized Transformations** (§3): Narrative → Narrative (P1, same-level semantic abstraction) and Narrative → Schematic (P2, same-level formalization);
- **Tokenized → Latent** (§4): P3, compressing discrete experience into continuous computational states;
- **Tokenized → Parametric** (§5): Tokenized → Evaluator (P4, solidifying experience as assessment capability) and Tokenized → Policy (P5, solidifying experience as decision-making capability);
- **Parametric-Source Transformations** (§6): Evaluator → Policy (P6, preference alignment) and Parametric → Tokenized (P7, externalizing implicit experience).

Beyond single pathways, we dedicate an independent chapter to **Composite Pipelines** (§7), anatomizing works that chain multiple pathways as an integrated method, identifying composition patterns and analyzing integration mechanisms. We then provide a **Cross-Pathway Synthesis** (§8) that compares pathway trade-offs, analyzes utilization drivers, and offers scenario-based recommendations, followed by **Open Problems and Future Directions** (§9).

Methodologically, our inclusion criteria require data to simultaneously exhibit decision-process semantics (mappable to $e = (c, a, o, f)$) and heterogeneous action spaces (actions belonging to an LLM-based system's heterogeneous action space). We exclude static corpus pretraining, single-step classification SFT, pure model distillation (Parametric → Parametric, where the experience semantic chain breaks), pure visual foundation model training, and non-LLM-based systems. Experience sources are unrestricted—agent self-generated, human demonstration, or teacher model synthesized—and judgments are based on underlying mechanisms rather than papers' self-positioning.

The main contributions of this survey are: (1) a unified framework centered on experience transformation, providing a shared analytical language for experience reuse across subfields of LLM-based agents; (2) a systematic treatment of 7 transformation pathways and their associated literature, revealing mechanism characteristics, trade-offs, and evolutionary trends; (3) identification and anatomy of composition patterns in composite pipelines, filling gaps that single-pathway analysis cannot cover; and (4) cross-pathway comparison with scenario-based recommendations, offering actionable guidance for method selection in future research.
