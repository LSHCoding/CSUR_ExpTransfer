# Anti-AI-Voice Rules For Survey Prose

Use this as a second-pass checklist after drafting the English LaTeX fragment. The goal is low density of generic LLM phrasing, not mechanical deletion of every abstract word.

## Rhythm

- Vary sentence length. Do not write three or more consecutive sentences with similar length.
- Use at most one chained parallel construction per paragraph, such as "not X, but Y", "not only X, but also Y", or "from X to Y".
- Do not pad two real points into a three-item list.
- Use em dashes sparingly and avoid semicolon chains.

## Word Choice

- Avoid LLM-signature words unless technically necessary: delve, tapestry, nuanced, underscore, pivotal, paramount, leverage as a verb, seamless, seamlessly, navigate figuratively, realm, intricate, multifaceted, robust unless technical, crucial, vital, essential, profound, meticulously, rich figuratively, vibrant, and landscape figuratively.
- Delete generic intensifiers such as key, core, fundamental, crucial, essential, central, deep, and deeper unless they carry specific technical meaning.
- Replace vague carrier verbs with concrete verbs. Prefer store, encode, compress, execute, index, replace, override, constrain, or expose over embody, capture, reflect, encompass, constitute, represent, manifest, or exhibit when the concrete verb fits.
- Avoid nominalized phrasing when a direct verb is available.
- Use grand framing nouns such as paradigm, lens, perspective, framework, dimension, and axis only when the term is operative in the argument.

## Argument

- Do not append empty balance such as "however, this approach has limitations" unless the limitation is named in the same sentence.
- Remove meta-commentary phrases: "it is worth noting that", "interestingly", "importantly", "notably", "remarkably", and "it should be emphasized that".
- Do not restate the paragraph in its final sentence. End with a consequence, transition, or stop after the point is made.
- Avoid decorative question setups such as "A central question is whether..." unless the next sentence answers the question.
- Cite with specificity. Avoid broad "prior work has explored X" claims followed by undifferentiated citation clusters.

## Survey-Specific Checks

- Avoid gap-filling cliches such as "Despite significant progress, X remains underexplored" and "To bridge this gap". State what is missing and why it matters.
- Comparative claims need a reference point: "better than Y under condition Z", not just "better".
- Do not announce contributions in body sections unless the source fragment is from the Introduction or Conclusion and explicitly does so.
- Do not copy planning labels such as "Core question:", "Essence:", or "Claim template:" into manuscript prose.
