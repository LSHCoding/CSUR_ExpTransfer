\section{Latent-Space Transformation: Tokenized-to-Latent Transformations}
\label{sec:tok2latent}

Latent-Space Transformation converts tokenized agent experience into a continuous representation that enters attention or hidden-state computation directly. The core reason to use a latent rather than a token is this: when a model processes a piece of tokenized experience, it forms an internal state that discrete tokens cannot fully recover---the layer-by-layer contextual representation, attention's organization of the history, the model's understanding after digesting the experience. Decoding the experience back to tokens and reading it in again discards that understanding. What Latent-Space Transformation reuses is exactly these internal representational states. It therefore sits between Tokenized and Parametric: the experience is no longer a readable symbol outside the model, yet not yet fixed into the weights.

Inference-optimization methods such as KV-cache eviction operate on KV/hidden states but carry no long-horizon experience-reuse semantics; we include only methods whose latent representation explicitly serves experience storage and reuse. By the mechanism required to obtain the latent representation, we distinguish three classes. \emph{Cache-Based} requires no training and reuses the real KV/hidden states from past forward passes directly. \emph{Prompt-Based} compresses experience into a static continuous prompt through training, with the backbone frozen. \emph{Module-Based} builds a dynamic latent-memory system---with write, read, and fusion---through training.

\subsection{Cache-Based Latent Transformation}

Cache-Based Latent Transformation requires no training; it saves and reuses the KV cache or hidden states produced in the model's past interactions directly. Memorizing Transformers~\cite{Wu22} established the mechanism: it writes the key--value pairs of earlier tokens at an intermediate layer into an external memory, and later time steps access them in attention through kNN retrieval. LAG~\cite{Che25b} applies this to a reasoning agent's self-generated experience: it runs a full forward pass over historical reasoning logs, persists the KV for only a small selected set of tokens, and at inference retrieves the relevant logs and injects their KV into the current reasoning. TempoFit~\cite{Sun26} pushes into embodied settings: on a VLA it stores the layer-wise KV of earlier time steps into memory, retrieves it, and injects it into the current attention, supporting the inheritance of history within a long-horizon episode.

\subsection{Prompt-Based Latent Transformation}

Prompt-Based Latent Transformation turns experience into a set of continuous vectors through training, with the backbone frozen. MAP-VLA~\cite{Li25g} segments expert demonstrations by task phase, trains a set of soft prompts for each phase, and at inference retrieves by trajectory similarity and adds them to the base prompt embedding. ReasonCACHE~\cite{Gup26} encodes large-scale reasoning demonstrations into trainable prefix K/V at each layer, concatenated with the current token KV into an extended attention dictionary. TokMem~\cite{Wu25c} encodes a procedural routine into a single memory-token embedding that serves both as a routable discrete index and as a continuous control vector, selected by a router per query.

\subsection{Module-Based Latent Transformation}

Module-Based Latent Transformation builds a latent-memory system that can write, read, and fuse dynamically: during interaction, historical observations and actions are encoded as latent entries that enter the memory; at inference, relevant entries are retrieved by the current state, and the read latents are fused---through cross-attention or gating---into the subsequent generation or action output.

Single-channel methods maintain one latent stream. LatentMem~\cite{Fu26} generates its latent only at query time: it retrieves multi-agent historical trajectories and has a role-aware composer compress them on the fly into a fixed-length latent matrix concatenated into the hidden states, so that the same experience can be re-represented under different agent roles. HAMLET~\cite{Koo25} uses learnable moment tokens to extract a latent summary at each step, aggregated by a shallow Transformer into a history-aware state. GMP~\cite{Gao26} has a binary memory gate decide whether the cached latent history passes through cross-attention into the policy. Chameleon~\cite{Guo26} uses selective state-space dynamics to compress multi-view visual and proprioceptive history into an episodic latent state injected into every layer of the policy.

Multi-channel methods split experience into several latent streams that carry different semantic roles, each maintained and read separately. MemoryVLA~\cite{Shi25} stores a visual perceptual latent and a linguistic cognitive latent separately, fusing them into action prediction through cross-attention and a gated residual. EchoVLA~\cite{Lin25d} maintains a persistent scene memory (a voxelized 3D feature volume) and a FIFO episodic memory, corresponding to the spatial and the short-range temporal dimensions. L\textsuperscript{2}-VMAS~\cite{Yu26d} splits multi-agent collaboration experience into a perception latent and a thinking latent, injected into the hidden states during high-entropy decoding.

\subsection{Discussion}

The differences among the three classes unfold along the Cache-Based $\rightarrow$ Prompt-Based $\rightarrow$ Module-Based progression. From front to back, implementation complexity and training investment rise, as do the length of history that can be held and the degree of dynamic adaptation; in the opposite direction, the readability and traceability of experience fall. The Cache-Based KV comes straight from a real forward pass and maps onto a concrete historical fragment; the Prompt-Based static vector is hard to map back to a single experience; the Module-Based dynamic memory is rewritten continually, and its provenance is the hardest to trace. From a broader view, Prompt-Based also sits between training-free in-context prompting and full fine-tuning, an intermediate position that matches its middle place among the three classes here.

Module-Based has several problems of its own: memory goes stale, old entries are overwritten once capacity saturates, the latent representation drifts with training, and a wrong experience, once entered, is hard to detect. These entries cannot be read and rewritten directly the way a Tokenized carrier can, so an error is hard to clean up in isolation once written. The higher the implementation complexity and the stronger the expressive power, the lower the inspectability and controllability.

All three classes currently concentrate in embodied and VLA settings, with markedly less application in pure-text reasoning agents. The reason lies in how tokenizable the experience is: visual, spatial, and proprioceptive experience is hard to write losslessly as discrete tokens, so latentization pays off most; textual reasoning experience can be kept in readable form directly, so the incentive to latentize is correspondingly weaker.
