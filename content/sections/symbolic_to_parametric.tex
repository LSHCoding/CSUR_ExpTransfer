\section{Tokenized-to-Parametric Transformations}
\label{sec:symbolic_to_parametric}

The transformations covered in this section share a destination: experience that lives in the tokenized layer---trajectories, demonstrations, preference annotations---is rewritten into the parametric layer, where it is no longer addressable as a stored record but is encoded into the model's weight distribution. Two pathways inhabit this destination, and they differ in the kind of parameterized capability they produce. P4 (Tokenized $\to$ Parametric Evaluator) trains a scoring function $V_\theta$ that takes an experience tuple and returns a verdict; P5 (Tokenized $\to$ Parametric Policy) trains a conditional distribution $\pi_\phi(a \mid c)$ that generates actions. The supervision form, the training procedure, and the way the resulting carrier is consumed at deployment all differ between the two, and this functional difference is exactly what motivates the subdivision of the parametric class into evaluator and policy parameters in \S\ref{sec:carriers}. The remainder of this section treats P4 in \S\ref{sec:p4} and P5 in \S\ref{sec:p5}, in each case organizing the body around the shape of the supervision that drives the training run.

\subsection{Tokenized-to-Evaluator Internalization}
\label{sec:p4}

P4 takes a corpus of experience tuples $(c,a,o,f)$ annotated with a correctness or preference signal and trains a parametric scoring function $V_\theta:(c,a,o,f)\to\mathbb{R}$ that scores unseen tuples at inference time. The annotation can come from human preference labels, from self-generated Monte Carlo rollouts whose empirical success rates serve as automatic per-step labels, or from rule-derived signals such as execution success or format correctness; the training objective is then a standard supervised or preference-style fine-tune on top of a language-model backbone. Because the resulting evaluator is no longer a locatable record but a forward-pass capability, what differs across works is the shape of the verdict it returns and the way the training data are constructed. The remainder of \S\ref{sec:p4} groups works by verdict granularity---an outcome-level scalar over a complete response (\S\ref{sec:p4_outcome}), a process-level signal placed on each intermediate step (\S\ref{sec:p4_process}), or a generative judgment that returns a written critique alongside a score (\S\ref{sec:p4_critic})---and then turns to multimodal realizations (\S\ref{sec:p4_multimodal}) and the discussion of common costs and weaknesses (\S\ref{sec:p4_discussion}).

\subsubsection{Mechanism}
\label{sec:p4_methods}

\paragraph{Outcome-level Reward Model.}
\label{sec:p4_outcome}

An outcome-level reward model assigns a single scalar to a complete response or trajectory and is trained either from human preference labels or from outcome-derived correctness signals. AgentRM fine-tunes a reward model over agent trajectories spanning nine task families and uses it at inference to guide the policy through Best-of-N sampling and step-level beam search; the resulting evaluator generalizes across tasks more reliably than a directly fine-tuned policy and carries a weak-to-strong property in which a smaller RM yields larger improvements when used to score outputs from a much larger policy model. Regularizing Hidden States retains the base model's language-modeling head and adds text-generation losses during reward-model training, so that the hidden states preserve their generative structure and the resulting RM is more stable under distribution shift. Listwise Reward Estimation replaces the standard pairwise preference loss with a listwise ranking objective, calibrating the reward jointly across the full set of candidate responses rather than only between pairs. Graph-Reward-SQL extends the outcome-level idea to Text-to-SQL by replacing scalar correctness with a graph-matching score that compares the predicted query's execution plan against the reference plan, so that the ``outcome'' the reward expresses is a structural similarity rather than a single bit of correctness.

\paragraph{Process-level Reward / Verifier (PRM).}
\label{sec:p4_process}

A process-level reward model---commonly called a PRM or process verifier---scores each intermediate step of a reasoning trajectory rather than only the final output, providing the dense gradient that an outcome-level scorer cannot. The dominant cost is per-step annotation, and the literature responds with two structurally divergent strategies. The Monte-Carlo strategy treats each prefix as an independent rollout candidate and uses empirical success rates as automatic step labels, as in Math-Shepherd, which constructs process-wise supervision data without human step annotation, and in SPARE, which uses a single reference solution to produce per-step alignment labels. The implicit strategy treats the step reward as a derivative of the outcome objective: by parameterizing outcome reward as the log-likelihood ratio between policy and reference, Free Process Rewards obtains step-level signal directly from response-level labels, and FreePRM derives pseudo step-level labels from outcome correctness alone. The implicit line is data-efficient by design and reports being competitive with MC-based PRMs while consuming a small fraction of their training data, but commits to a specific functional form for the reward that limits the expressible step-level structure. Several works fill out the space between these poles---SPARK, From Outcomes to Processes, Dyve with its fast-and-slow-thinking split, and SRR-Judge---and Reward-SQL extends the process-level formulation to Text-to-SQL through a Chain-of-CTEs decomposition. SWE-PRM applies the same per-step idea to long-horizon software-engineering trajectories with taxonomy-guided feedback that course-corrects an SWE agent before it strays.

\paragraph{Critic / Judge Models.}
\label{sec:p4_critic}

A critic or judge model expands the evaluator's output beyond a scalar to include a generated explanation, a corrected step, or a multi-axis verdict, so that the carrier becomes a function whose codomain combines a score with text rather than reducing to $\mathbb{R}$. SWEET-RL trains a step-level critic for collaborative reasoning tasks; From Novice to Expert builds a discriminator that separates expert from novice trajectories on the basis of step-level features; Self-Improving VLM Judges iteratively trains a multimodal judge whose verdicts then feed back into further policy refinement; PROPA trains a process-level reward model to provide step-level supervision for tool-augmented reasoning. Read as P4 instances in isolation, these works contribute generative or multi-axis evaluator designs; how each of them subsequently uses the evaluator to drive further training is taken up later in the survey (\S\ref{sec:composite}), where the relevant pipeline can be analyzed as a unit.

\subsubsection{Multimodal Realization}
\label{sec:p4_multimodal}

Multimodal P4 is sparser and structurally harder than its text-only counterpart, because step-level supervision must now span both visual perception and textual reasoning, and per-step correctness in visual reasoning chains is rarely directly computable. GM-PRM is the most developed instance currently in the literature: rather than emitting a passive scalar at each step, it evaluates every step along three axes---step intent, visual alignment, and logical soundness. When the first erroneous step is identified, GM-PRM generates a corrected version of that step, turning the evaluator from a passive judge into a constructive collaborator whose output enters a Refined Best-of-N selection so that its contribution at inference is not only filtering but also revision. Beyond GM-PRM, multimodal evaluators in the current literature appear largely as components inside larger training procedures rather than as stand-alone P4 contributions, and the underlying bottleneck across these cases is uniform: obtaining reliable step-level correctness signals on multimodal trajectories without prohibitively expensive expert annotation.

\subsubsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p4_discussion}

A P4 evaluator pays its cost almost entirely on the supervision side: every training run consumes labeled experience tuples, and the process-level variant amplifies this by demanding labels at every intermediate position rather than only at the trajectory's end. The Monte-Carlo and implicit-PRM strategies discussed earlier are direct responses to this pressure---the first replaces human step-labels with automatically estimated success rates, the second derives step-level signal from outcome supervision alone---but neither escapes the underlying fact that the evaluator's reliability is set by what its training signal can actually discriminate. The relevant baseline for the gain side is not raw experience but a training-free rule-based scorer such as execution success, answer matching, or format validity. Compared to that baseline, a trained evaluator buys two things: it covers situations where no rule-based check exists, namely open-ended interactions whose correctness is not closed-form, and it returns continuous, calibrated scores that support Best-of-$N$ selection, beam search, and gradient-based policy improvement, where the rule-based alternative is binary or sparse and saturates quickly. The trade-off is therefore best read as a question of how much supervision the field is willing to invest in to obtain a finer-grained signal, rather than as a binary choice between evaluator and no evaluator, and the cost curve through which that investment translates into reliability is the practical lever each subgroup of works is operating on.

Two weaknesses recur across deployed P4 evaluators. The first is reward hacking: when the evaluator has bounded coverage but the policy is allowed to optimize against it without restraint, the policy finds inputs the evaluator scores highly and a human reviewer would not, and the standard mitigations---KL regularization to a reference model, periodic refresh of the training data with new policy rollouts---reduce the rate without dissolving the underlying tension between a finite-capacity scoring function and an open-ended optimizer; the Regularizing Hidden States line of work is an engineering response in this direction, constraining the RM not to collapse into a degenerate scorer but not removing hacking in principle. The second is the brittleness of process-level evaluators on out-of-distribution tasks: a PRM trained on, say, mathematical chain-of-thought tends to lose calibration on coding step-by-step or on multimodal reasoning, because step-level signal is more tightly coupled to a particular style of intermediate output than outcome-level signal is. Both weaknesses are partly engineering and partly structural: better data collection and better regularizers help, but neither closes the gap entirely, and reusing one P4 evaluator across substantially different task families remains an open practical concern.

P4 evaluators are utilized at inference time through Score-and-Select/Train, catalogued among the utilization modes of \S\ref{sec:cross_pathway:utilization:modes}.



\subsection{Tokenized-to-Policy Internalization}
\label{sec:p5}

P5 takes experience trajectories---expert demonstrations, agent self-generated rollouts paired with reward signals, or filtered self-play data---and trains the conditional action distribution $\pi_\phi(a\mid c)$ that the model executes at inference. The carrier change is the most irreversible in the survey: the experience is no longer addressable as a stored record but becomes the policy itself, and once the training run is complete there is no granular handle through which a single training example can be revised or removed. P5 is the most populous pathway among the seven defined here. The works partition primarily by what supplies the gradient signal---direct supervision against demonstrated actions, scalar reward from the environment or a designed rule, signals from a separately trained evaluator, or self-generated targets that the policy learns to imitate---and not by the optimizer itself, since SFT, GRPO, PPO, and DPO all leave the carrier within $\pi$-Par. The last two of these supervision sources always involve a pathway upstream of P5 (a separate evaluator training run, or a self-generation loop that closes back on the policy), so their pipelines are taken up as units in \S\ref{sec:composite}; the body of \S\ref{sec:p5} concentrates on direct and reward-based supervision (\S\ref{sec:p5_direct}, \S\ref{sec:p5_reward}), then turns to multimodal realizations (\S\ref{sec:p5_multimodal}) and the discussion of common costs and weaknesses (\S\ref{sec:p5_discussion}). SFT-then-RL training curricula are kept inside this section because no carrier-type change occurs across the curriculum stages.

\subsubsection{Mechanism}
\label{sec:p5_methods}

\paragraph{Direct Supervision (SFT on Demonstrations).}
\label{sec:p5_direct}

The most direct form of P5 trains the policy on demonstrated trajectories with supervised fine-tuning, the gradient signal coming from the demonstrated actions themselves. ATLaS identifies the steps in expert trajectories that are most decision-critical and fine-tunes the policy only on those, reporting that fine-tuning on roughly thirty per cent of the steps outperforms fine-tuning on all of them and isolating ``selection of supervision'' as a non-trivial design lever. E$^2$CL incorporates exploration-induced errors alongside expert demonstrations so that the policy learns both the correct continuation and how to recover from observed mistakes. Learning From Failure conditions the input on a success or failure prefix token, training a single policy that can be queried for either an attempt-and-succeed or an attempt-and-fail style of generation depending on the prefix supplied. SDFT removes the need for an external teacher altogether by having the model produce demonstrations under a structured prompting protocol and then fine-tune on its own output. Watch Every Step (IPR) augments SFT with iteratively constructed contrastive action pairs whose relative quality is estimated through Monte Carlo rollouts; the rollouts are computed in line rather than stored as a separate evaluator checkpoint, so the work remains within direct-supervision P5 even though its construction of the supervision set borrows ideas from process-level evaluation.

\paragraph{Reward-based Supervision (RL with Environmental or Rule-based Reward).}
\label{sec:p5_reward}

A second group trains the policy with policy-gradient methods---GRPO, PPO, or related---using scalar reward signals that come from the environment, a verifier, or a designed rule. ToolRL conducts a comprehensive study of reward design for tool-selection and tool-application tasks under GRPO and shows that a sufficiently structured rule-based reward (answer matching combined with sub-task decomposition) closes much of the gap that would otherwise motivate training a separate reward model; because the reward is rule-based rather than learned, ToolRL belongs within atomic P5 rather than within an evaluator-mediated pipeline. ARTIST trains the policy to autonomously decide when, how, and which tools to invoke within multi-turn reasoning chains using outcome-based RL, with tool calls treated as first-class actions in the action space. SPA-RL targets the credit-assignment problem directly by decomposing the final reward into stepwise contributions through a stepwise progress-attribution scheme, providing the optimizer with denser feedback without training an explicit per-step evaluator. ReTool combines an SFT cold-start with PPO that drives the dynamic interleaving of code execution and natural-language reasoning across multiple turns; DeepAnalyze applies the same SFT-then-GRPO curriculum to data-science tasks; Agentic Memory exposes memory operations as tool-style actions and trains the policy with step-wise GRPO designed to handle the sparse and discontinuous rewards that arise when memory writes have no immediate effect on task success. Across this group, the SFT-then-RL curriculum keeps the carrier type unchanged across stages---the SFT checkpoint remains a policy parameter, and the subsequent RL run continues to update the same parameter set---so the curriculum is not a cross-pathway composition but a sequenced training procedure within P5.

\subsubsection{Multimodal Realization}
\label{sec:p5_multimodal}

When the source experience comes from a GUI agent, P5 has a structurally favourable starting point. The action space is intrinsically discrete and well-structured---click, scroll, type, key-press, navigate---and the task-completion or state-matching signal is often directly computable, so both pure SFT and reward-based RL apply without bridging machinery. InfiGUIAgent trains a GUI policy through a two-stage SFT pipeline whose first stage instills GUI understanding and grounding and whose second stage introduces hierarchical reasoning and expectation--reflection reasoning. TongUI scales the data side by mining online GUI tutorials---both videos and articles---into a large trajectory corpus and then fine-tuning a Qwen2.5-VL backbone on that corpus, with the data construction rather than the optimizer carrying the contribution. UI-TARS-2 places a stabilized multi-turn RL procedure on top of a hybrid GUI environment that integrates file systems and terminals; the multi-turn RL component is one of the more developed engineering responses currently in the literature to the long-horizon credit-assignment instability that multi-turn GUI rollouts exhibit.

The opposite picture appears in embodied vision-language-action (VLA) training, whose raw experience consists of motor commands, joint-angle trajectories, or velocity profiles defined over continuous spaces, and whose reward signals are scarce by default. The literature responds by manufacturing dense feedback. EVOLVE-VLA introduces a learned progress estimator that produces dense feedback for VLA training in the absence of an oracle reward, and pairs it with an accumulative progress-estimation curriculum and progressive horizon extension so that the policy is trained on increasingly long-horizon tasks under denser-than-oracle feedback; whether the estimator constitutes an independent evaluator parameter or is best read as an in-line auxiliary head is a borderline question taken up below. LongNav-R1 reformulates long-horizon navigation as continuous multi-turn conversation and trains the policy with horizon-adaptive policy optimization that adjusts to the instability induced by lengthening horizons. Reflective Planning combines a pretrained vision-language model with reflection-style self-correction during planning, so that the policy emits not only an action but a brief reasoning artifact that is consumed in the next decision. MAI-UI and OmegaUse extend the SFT-plus-RL recipe to mobile, desktop, and mixture-of-experts backbone settings, reflecting that progress in multimodal P5 still moves largely through extension of an existing recipe rather than through new optimization machinery.

\subsubsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p5_discussion}

Compared to the simplest available alternative---retrieving a relevant raw trajectory and prepending it as in-context examples---P5 internalization purchases two compounding gains. The experience is no longer prepended on every call, so long-context costs vanish and the saving compounds over query volume; and a policy trained on a diverse body of trajectories generalizes to nearby tasks for which no individual stored trajectory would have matched on retrieval. The cost direction is equally clear: training is required, though parameter-efficient methods, distillation, and batch optimization steadily lower the bill; high-quality demonstration data is needed and remains the dominant recurring cost in the SFT setting, with the partial relief from self-generated data taken up in \S\ref{sec:composite}. The cost that does not yield to engineering is editability: once experience has been encoded into a weight delta, no general-purpose mechanism revises a single training example after the fact, and this is the structural reason the field's response to obsolete or contradicted experience is to retrain rather than to edit, and why the reverse externalization pathway P7 (\S\ref{sec:p7}) exists at all.

Three weaknesses recur across deployed P5 systems and divide cleanly by training regime. SFT-trained policies are subject to catastrophic forgetting: a fine-tune on a new task degrades performance on previously learned tasks, and the severity scales with the divergence between the new and prior task distributions; LoRA-style adapters, replay buffers, and multi-task batching reduce the rate without removing the underlying tension between weight update and weight preservation. RL-trained policies are subject to policy collapse: without sufficient diversity preservation the action distribution loses entropy, exploration ceases, and reward maximization converges on a narrow output mode; reward-design studies and dynamic-sampling schemes attenuate the collapse but cannot remove it, because reward maximization is by construction a converging operator. The third weakness, multi-turn RL instability, sits between the structural and the engineering: long-horizon credit assignment combined with multi-turn rollout variance produces training runs that are difficult to stabilize, and the line of engineering responses---UI-TARS-2's stabilized multi-turn procedure, MobileGUI-RL's trajectory-aware advantages, LongNav-R1's horizon-adaptive policy optimization---has steadily improved the situation, but a structural floor remains because the underlying credit-assignment problem itself does not admit a closed-form remedy.

P5 policies are utilized at inference time through Forward-and-Generate, catalogued among the utilization modes of \S\ref{sec:cross_pathway:utilization:modes}.

The two pathways collected in this section both internalize tokenized experience into the parametric layer, but they produce different parameterized capabilities: P4 yields an evaluator that returns verdicts, while P5 yields a policy that generates actions. Their cost profiles consequently diverge---P4 is bound primarily by the supervision needed to make the evaluator reliable, P5 by the volume and quality of the demonstration or reward data needed to fit a generative policy---and the engineering curves through which these costs translate into reliability are not parallel.
