\section{Parametric-Source Transformations}
\label{sec:parametric_centered}

Section~\ref{sec:symbolic_to_parametric} covered how tokenized experience enters the parametric layer---as an evaluator that returns verdicts or as a policy that generates actions. The present section treats two transformations whose source carrier is already parametric, and they move in opposite directions along the carrier spectrum. The first runs forward: an evaluator parameter set, itself the product of a prior transformation, supplies a scoring signal that drives a further weight update on a policy parameter set, so that experience flows between two different parameterized forms without leaving the parametric layer. The second runs backward: a policy whose weights already encode experience is decoded back into explicit tokenized artifacts---synthetic trajectories, verification checklists, structured workflows---recovering interpretable, storable form from opacity. The two pathways differ sharply in how much independent literature they have attracted---the first almost none, for structural reasons discussed below; the second about five works with a rapidly growing GUI data-synthesis sub-line---and this asymmetry is itself a finding. Section~\ref{sec:p6} treats the forward case and its structural peculiarities; Section~\ref{sec:p7} treats the backward case and the works that populate it.

\subsection{Evaluator-to-Policy Internalization}
\label{sec:p6}

\subsubsection{Mechanism}
\label{sec:p6_mechanism}

P6 takes a trained evaluator $V_\theta$---itself the product of an earlier transformation that encoded annotated experience tuples into parameters---and uses the scores it emits to update the weights of a policy $\pi_\phi$ through gradient-based optimization, most commonly RLHF, DPO, or PPO. The carrier change is from evaluator parameters to policy parameters, and what makes this pathway structurally unique among all seven is that the two roles normally separated in a transformation---producing a new carrier and consuming it---are here performed by a single operation. The evaluator is utilized by running a forward pass that returns a verdict on the policy's output, and that very verdict simultaneously serves as the training signal that drives the transformation. This dual nature does not threaten the pathway's legitimacy: the anchor to experience is preserved through transitivity of the derivation chain (the evaluator was trained on trajectories that satisfy Condition~1), and the experience is embodied in the resulting policy through the RLHF signal chain (the evaluator participates by scoring $(c,a)$ pairs, so the semantic chain is unbroken, satisfying Condition~2). The distinction from pure parametric-to-parametric weight transfer---distillation or model merging---is that in those cases no carrier with experience semantics mediates the transfer, and the chain of experience grounding breaks; here the evaluator re-instantiates that grounding on every forward pass.

P6 is the only pathway among the seven for which essentially no independent work exists. Every V-Par$\to\pi$-Par instance in the literature covered by this survey appears as one step inside an Evaluate-then-Optimize composite pipeline, in which the evaluator is first trained from tokenized experience (P4) and only then used to update the policy (P6), with both steps reported as a single end-to-end contribution. Three mutually reinforcing factors explain this absence. The first is distribution shift: a reward model trained on rollouts from one policy distribution loses reliability as the policy moves under its own optimization, so a frozen evaluator driving an unbounded policy optimization hits the reward-over-optimization ceiling quickly, and a pure-P6 contribution that freezes the evaluator would have a short useful lifetime by construction. The second is publication convention: the RLHF community evaluates the end-to-end policy, not the intermediate evaluator, and the contribution is usually framed as ``better alignment'' rather than as ``better evaluator-to-policy transfer,'' so isolating the P6 step offers little incentive under existing reporting norms. The third is engineering economy: once the evaluator is in hand and distribution shift is expected, interleaving evaluator retraining (re-entering P4) with policy updates (re-entering P6) consistently outperforms a single P6 pass, and the composite replaces the atomic step. Together these three factors make the absence of independent P6 work a structural finding rather than a literature-search failure; the composite form is taken up on its own terms in the Evaluate-then-Optimize pattern.

\subsubsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p6_discussion}

The relevant baseline for P6 is not raw experience but outcome-based reinforcement learning---using a sparse, environment-supplied signal such as task completion, answer matching, or execution success to drive policy-gradient updates, without training an intermediate evaluator. Against that baseline, what P6 buys is signal quality: the evaluator returns continuous, calibrated scores instead of binary verdicts; it can supply process-level feedback at intermediate steps instead of only at trajectory endpoints; and it covers open-ended interactions for which no rule-based outcome signal exists. These properties translate into measured policy improvement when the scores are consumed through Best-of-$N$ selection, beam search, or step-level credit assignment. The cost is two stacked bills: the evaluator training cost already analyzed under evaluator internalization, plus the P6 optimization cost---multi-round on-policy rollouts, gradient steps, and the compute needed to keep the evaluator's training distribution aligned with the moving policy. A cost unique to this pathway is evaluator-bias amplification: when the evaluator systematically overrates a certain class of $(c,a)$ pairs, the policy gradient steers the policy into dense exploration of exactly that class, and the evaluator has no internal mechanism to detect or correct its own bias; in outcome-based RL the reward function is a fixed environment signal, so this kind of cascading self-deception does not arise.

Because P6 almost never appears as an isolated contribution, the direct empirical evidence for the two weaknesses described below comes from evaluator behavior observed inside Evaluate-then-Optimize composite pipelines rather than from standalone P6 ablations. The first weakness is reward over-optimization: a bounded-capacity evaluator facing an open-ended policy optimizer will eventually be gamed---the policy finds inputs the evaluator scores highly and a human reviewer would not---and while KL regularization to a reference model and periodic evaluator retraining reduce the rate, they cannot dissolve the underlying tension between a finite scoring function and an unbounded optimizer. The second weakness is evaluator--policy distribution shift: as the policy moves under its own RL updates, the distribution of its outputs drifts away from the distribution on which the evaluator was trained, and the evaluator's calibration degrades; this one is substantially engineering in nature, since periodic on-policy retraining of the evaluator can restore calibration, but doing so adds to the already stacked training cost. The two weaknesses differ in how much engineering can close the gap: distribution shift yields to better pipeline design, reward over-optimization does not---and this difference is exactly what sets the core design tension in Evaluate-then-Optimize composites, where the relative frequency of evaluator retraining versus policy updating becomes the central lever.

When a P6 step occurs inside an Evaluate-then-Optimize pipeline, the evaluator is utilized through Score-and-Select/Train (\S\ref{sec:cross_pathway:utilization:modes}), with the critical property that the utilization itself is simultaneously the transformation driver.

\subsection{Parametric-to-Tokenized Externalization}
\label{sec:p7}

\subsubsection{Mechanism}
\label{sec:p7_overview}

Human-labeled experience data is expensive to produce and limited in coverage. As agents are deployed across increasingly diverse environments, the demand for training data quickly outstrips what manual annotation can supply, and using a model that has already internalized useful behavior as a generator of synthetic data becomes a practical response. The P7 pathway captures this operation in the carrier taxonomy: a parametric carrier---typically a trained policy---acts as a generator that externalizes weight-encoded experience into explicit, editable tokenized content consumed by downstream systems as trajectories, rubrics, or executable workflows. This is the inverse of the operation that P5 performs: where P5 encodes experience into weights through gradient updates backed by the general-purpose machinery of backpropagation, P7 reconstructs tokenized content from weights with no corresponding universal engine. Every published P7 work instead relies on a deliberately engineered generation procedure, and this absence of a general mechanism is the structural reason the pathway is sparsely populated. The five works that constitute the current P7 literature divide naturally by how the artifact is consumed: three produce raw-trajectory-like data for downstream supervised fine-tuning of a different agent, and two produce structured or semi-structured artifacts deployed directly as verifiers or executable workflows. A boundary must be drawn between P7 and the Self-Generation Loop: when the externalized artifact is fed back to retrain the same policy that generated it, the resulting cycle belongs to the composite-pipeline analysis; P7 proper is defined by the artifact's consumer being a different agent, or the artifact itself being the terminal product. The remainder of this section groups the works by this consumption axis, then turns to multimodal realizations and the discussion of shared costs and characteristic weaknesses.

\subsubsection{Literature Groups}
\label{sec:p7_groups}

\paragraph{Data Synthesis for Downstream Training.}

Three works produce trajectory-like tokenized data for supervised fine-tuning of a model different from the one that generated it, with the policy deployed as an environment-interaction engine and its output collected as training corpus. OS-Genesis reverses the conventional trajectory-collection workflow: instead of executing a pre-defined task, the agent first perceives the environment and performs step-wise interactions freely, then retrospectively derives high-quality task descriptions from those interactions, with a trajectory reward model filtering for quality---so the trajectory defines the task rather than the task constraining the trajectory. GUI-ReWalk approaches the same problem from the opposite direction, beginning with stochastic exploration that emulates human trial-and-error to maximize interaction diversity and progressively transitioning into a reasoning-guided phase in which inferred goals drive coherent, purposeful sequences; its two-phase design buys diversity without sacrificing structure, and its multi-stride task generation constructs long-horizon cross-application workflows. BugPilot transports the synthesis idea from GUI to code: a SWE agent is instructed to introduce a feature into a codebase and may unintentionally break existing tests in the process, yielding bugs whose distribution more closely matches real development patterns than bugs created by local perturbation. The property shared by all three is that the model performing the externalization and the model being trained on the output are different---the synthesis loop is open, with no return arrow feeding back into the externalizer, and this open-loop structure is the boundary that excludes Self-Generation Loop works. A second shared property is structural rather than definitional: every work in this subgroup depends on a computable fidelity signal---OS-Genesis's trajectory reward model, GUI-ReWalk's intent-aware state matching, BugPilot's test breakage---and this is not coincidental; an automatic quality check is the structural precondition for data-synthesis P7 to function at all.

\paragraph{Structural and Semantic Externalization.}

In this second group the externalized artifact is not training data but a deployable product consumed directly by an agent, a workflow engine, or a verification process. EvoAgentX represents the strongest formalization within P7: it externalizes policy knowledge about multi-agent orchestration into a complete schematic-tokenized workflow whose topology, agent roles, and tool configurations are explicitly represented, and it iteratively refines this workflow through integrated evolutionary optimization algorithms---TextGrad, AFlow, and MIPRO---so that the resulting artifact is not a one-shot decode but an evolved structure; its reuse mode is direct execution by a multi-agent execution system. Agentic Rubrics occupies a lighter point on the formalization spectrum: a policy serving as an expert SWE agent interacts with a repository and produces a context-grounded rubric checklist---natural-language criteria, one per line---which is then used to score candidate patches without test execution; the rubric is narrative-tokenized in form but functionally substitutes for what would otherwise be a trained evaluator. This dual nature is worth marking: the rubric is a tokenized artifact---storable, editable, inspectable line by line---that performs an evaluator's function, so it sits at a boundary where carrier form and functional role diverge. In this survey it is classified by carrier form under P7, while its evaluator function is noted separately in the utilization analysis. Although this subgroup currently holds only two representatives, their contrast already sketches two diverging paths for structural externalization---one toward strong formalization with executable guarantees and high schema-design cost, the other toward lighter formalization with flexibility and editability at the price of scoring consistency---and this divergence structurally echoes the narrative-versus-schematic split already seen within the tokenized-internal pathways.

\subsubsection{Multimodal Realization}
\label{sec:p7_multimodal}

Three of the five current P7 works target GUI domains---OS-Genesis and GUI-ReWalk directly, while Agentic Rubrics operates in the adjacent SWE domain whose action space shares the same discrete, structured character---and this concentration is not accidental. GUI interaction possesses three structural properties that together make it a particularly favourable target for parametric-to-tokenized externalization. First, the GUI action space is intrinsically discrete and well-typed (click, scroll, type, navigate), so synthesized trajectories are legal token sequences by construction and require no discretization of continuous signals. Second, task completion and state matching in GUI environments are often directly computable---a widget appears, a URL changes, a page state is reached---which supplies an automatic quality filter for the synthesized output; OS-Genesis's trajectory reward model and GUI-ReWalk's intent-aware reasoning both exploit this computability. Third, GUI interaction naturally generates records in two channels simultaneously---visual screenshots and textual representations such as accessibility trees, DOM structures, or natural-language instructions---so the synthesized trajectory can carry multimodal richness while remaining retrievable through the text channel via semantic search; this dual-channel property has no counterpart in either embodied continuous control or pure text domains. GUI is therefore the most favourable domain for this pathway at its current stage of development, and the core difficulty of extending P7 to other modalities is precisely the absence of these three properties elsewhere.

Outside GUI, P7 is sparse to the point of near-absence. BugPilot is the only representative in the code domain; embodied control, audio, and pure text reasoning currently have no independent P7 instance. The obstacles are structural rather than contingent. In embodied control, the action space is continuous, making trajectory quality hard to verify automatically---there is no analogue of the page-state-matching check that anchors GUI synthesis---and the visual complexity of embodied scenes combined with the scarcity of reward signals makes generating faithful trajectories an order of magnitude harder than in GUI. BugPilot succeeds in the code domain precisely because code shares with GUI the one property that current P7 techniques appear to depend on: the outcome of a modification is computable. Tests pass or fail, diffs are parseable, and that computable signal---test breakage, in BugPilot's case---serves the same role as page-state matching in GUI P7, namely an automatic fidelity check that does not require human judgment. Pure text reasoning has no equivalent computable fidelity signal, so P7 instances in that domain are currently zero, and this absence is recorded as a falsifiable prediction in the open-problems section.

\subsubsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p7_discussion}

The natural baseline for P7 is human annotation or existing public datasets---obtaining trajectories, demonstrations, or verification artifacts from human effort rather than from policy externalization. Against that baseline, P7 buys a fundamental shift in cost structure: the marginal cost of one additional human-labeled trajectory is roughly constant because it costs human time, while the marginal cost of one additional synthesized trajectory is an inference pass that can be amortized over batch generation. In regimes that demand large volumes of high-coverage trajectory data---GUI agent training that must span hundreds of interface layouts and interaction patterns---the compute-for-data economics of P7 can become more attractive than human-for-data collection by volume. The binding constraint is the quality ceiling: the fidelity and diversity of the externalized artifact are bounded above by the capability of the policy that generates it, and a policy that performs modestly on a task distribution cannot synthesize trajectories that surpass its own competence; BugPilot's bug diversity and GUI-ReWalk's interaction variety can be pushed closer to that ceiling through strategy design, but they cannot break through it. Information-theoretically, P7 is a lossy reconstruction---weight-encoded experience is unfolded into explicit tokenized content, but the reconstruction has no inverse-validation mechanism analogous to backpropagation's loss function, so the product cannot be automatically verified at generation time; the quality-filtering components attached to each work (OS-Genesis's trajectory reward model, BugPilot's test execution, GUI-ReWalk's intent-aware reasoning) are engineering compensations for this structural absence. The cost-gain structure of P7 can therefore be summarized as low marginal cost per unit of data against a hard quality ceiling set by the generating policy's own ability; this ceiling is higher in domains where current policies are already strong (GUI) and lower where they are weak (embodied continuous control), and the difference between these two ceilings is the economic explanation for the uneven domain distribution observed in the multimodal analysis.

The weaknesses of P7 fall into three tiers by how tractable they are. The first is incomplete distribution coverage: the externalizing policy generates interaction patterns it already handles well and avoids boundary cases it has not seen or struggles with, leaving long-tail scenarios under-represented in the synthesized data. This can be substantially mitigated through design---GUI-ReWalk's stochastic exploration phase and OS-Genesis's reverse task synthesis are direct engineering responses to exactly this problem---but the injected diversity is bounded by the design space of the injection strategy and cannot guarantee coverage of genuinely unknown edge cases. The second weakness is hallucinated trajectories: the policy may synthesize interaction sequences that look locally plausible but are globally inconsistent with the environment they claim to describe. In GUI domains these hallucinations can be caught and filtered automatically because state matching is computable; in domains that lack a closed-form fidelity check, hallucinated trajectories silently enter the dataset and cause quality damage downstream that is hard to trace back to its source. The third and deepest weakness is structural: when no automatic fidelity check exists, the quality-assurance chain for P7 externalization breaks entirely, and distinguishing a faithful trajectory from a hallucinated one requires human judgment---at which point P7 loses its defining economic advantage and collapses into an expensive semi-automated scheme. This dilemma currently has no known engineering exit and is the fundamental obstacle to extending P7 beyond GUI. Model collapse under iterative self-consumption is not directly observed in the current P7 pool because all five works maintain an open loop with different generator and trainee models; the closed-loop case belongs to the Self-Generation Loop discussion in the composite-pipelines chapter. The success of GUI-ReWalk and OS-Genesis in the GUI domain should not be read as proof of P7's general viability; it demonstrates viability strictly conditional on the availability of a computable fidelity signal, and that condition is absent in every other domain where P7 has been attempted.

P7 artifacts return to the tokenized layer and are utilized through Retrieve-and-Prepend when they are raw trajectories consumed as training data, through Retrieve-and-Execute when they are executable workflows such as EvoAgentX's output, and through Score-and-Select when they function as verifiers such as Agentic Rubrics' checklist; the mapping from artifact form to utilization mode is many-to-many and is treated systematically in the cross-pathway synthesis (\S\ref{sec:cross_pathway:utilization:modes}).

The two pathways collected in this section close the loop on the parametric carrier's role in the transformation network, approaching it from opposite directions---P6 as the forward flow of experience between two parameterized forms, P7 as the reverse recovery of parameterized experience back into explicit tokenized artifacts. That one of them almost never occurs independently and the other is tightly constrained to domains with computable fidelity checks is a descriptive result of the current literature, not a gap to be filled by forced coverage. Both of these structural facts are taken up in the composite-pipelines analysis that follows: the Evaluate-then-Optimize pattern is where the forward flow actually lives, and the Self-Generation Loop is where externalized artifacts are re-integrated into a closed cycle whose full structure requires treating the loop as a unit.
