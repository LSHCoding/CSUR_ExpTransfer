# Academic English Writing Rules for Survey Translation

## 1. Conciseness

1. **Delete redundancy**: Words that can be removed without changing meaning. "due to the fact that" → "because", "In the form of" → "As", "In many cases" → "Often", "has the ability to" → "can".
2. **Avoid nominalization**: Don't bury actions in nouns. Use real verbs. "to effect an installation" → "to install", "to conduct an analysis" → "to analyze", "to give consideration" → "to consider".
3. **Don't bury the subject in prepositional phrases**: "The use of this method would eliminate the problem" → "This method would eliminate the problem".
4. **Keep sentences short**: Long sentences harm readability. Information is more persuasive in concise, precise writing.

## 2. Clarity

1. **Prefer active voice**: "The researcher conducted the study" not "The study was conducted by the researcher". Passive is acceptable in Methods sections.
2. **Information placement**: Put given/known information at the start, new/complex information at the end of the sentence.
3. **Parallelism**: Keep coordinated elements in the same grammatical form. "reasoning, planning, and tool use" (all nouns) — don't mix nouns with gerunds.
4. **Modifiers near what they modify**: Avoid dangling modifiers.

## 3. Consistency

- Hyphens, units, punctuation, grammar, symbols, capitalization, abbreviations: keep consistent throughout.
- First occurrence of a term: give full name + abbreviation (e.g., "Large Language Model (LLM)"), then use the abbreviation consistently.
- Don't alternate between different translations for the same concept.

## 4. Common Chinglish Errors

1. **Extra nouns and verbs**: Chinese authors often add unnecessary words. "A steady growth in GDP is necessary for attainment of economic prosperity" → drop "attainment".
2. **Redundant twins**: "various and different methods" → "various methods", "final and ultimate goal" → "final goal", "basic and fundamental concept" → "fundamental concept".
3. **Sentence construction**: Never use "Although ... but ..." or "Because ... so ..." (English allows only one conjunction).
4. **Empty intensifiers**: Avoid "very", "quite", "really", "obviously", "clearly" — delete or replace with precise adverbs (e.g., "substantially", "markedly").

## 5. Tense and Person

| Section | Recommended Tense |
|---------|-------------------|
| Abstract | Present simple (what the paper does) |
| Introduction | Present simple (state of the field) |
| Related Work / citing others | Past simple or Present perfect ("Wang et al. proposed...", "Recent studies have shown...") |
| Describing this survey's organization | Present simple ("Section 3 presents...") |
| Methods / experiments | Past simple (passive acceptable) |
| Conclusion | Present simple + Present perfect |

**Person**: "We" is widely accepted in surveys ("We classify ... into three categories"). Avoid "I" and "you".

## 6. Citation Rules

1. First mention: use full author names or surname + initial, with "et al." for ≥3 authors.
2. Method/algorithm names: capitalize consistently (e.g., "AutoSurvey", "SurveyForge").
3. Don't cite work you haven't read directly (no "second-hand citations").
4. Core survey paragraphs should have citations every 1–2 sentences, but don't pile up [1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8] — pick the 2–3 most representative.
5. Follow target journal formatting (ACM, IEEE, Springer LNCS, etc.).
